Understanding Power of Attorney in Africa

A Power of Attorney (POA) is a legal document authorising one person (the attorney) to act on behalf of another (the principal) in legal, financial, or personal matters. In Africa, POA requirements vary significantly by country — some require notarisation, others require registration or stamping by the relevant authority.

  • General POA: Grants broad authority to manage all or most aspects of the principal's affairs.
  • Special/Limited POA: Restricts the attorney's powers to specific, defined transactions — such as selling a single property or operating a specific bank account.
  • Enduring/Durable POA: Remains valid even if the principal becomes incapacitated. Available in South Africa, Kenya, and Ghana.

For property transactions in Nigeria, Uganda, and Tanzania, a POA must typically be registered with the lands registry. In South Africa, the High Court or a notary public must certify the document. Always verify the current requirements with a practising attorney in your jurisdiction.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does a Power of Attorney need to be notarised?
Notarisation requirements vary by country and the type of transaction. For banking, many banks in Africa require a notarised or commissioned POA. For property transactions, most countries require notarisation AND registration with the relevant lands or deeds office. For general use, a witnessed POA signed before a Commissioner of Oaths is often sufficient.
Can a Power of Attorney be used abroad?
Yes, but the document may need to be apostilled (for Hague Convention countries) or legalised through the relevant embassy. Countries like South Africa, Kenya, and Tanzania are members of the Hague Convention, making apostilles widely recognised.
Can I revoke a Power of Attorney?
Yes. A POA can be revoked at any time by the principal while they are of sound mind. Revocation should be done in writing, and where the POA was registered, the revocation should also be registered. The attorney must be notified of the revocation in writing.
What is the difference between a POA and a mandate?
While often used interchangeably in common parlance, a mandate is typically a narrower authority given to an agent to perform a specific task, whereas a POA is a formal legal instrument that is registered and has specific statutory requirements. In civil law countries like Côte d'Ivoire and Senegal, the mandate (mandat) is the primary concept used under French-influenced law.

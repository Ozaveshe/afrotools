How to calculate percentages

A percentage is a fraction expressed out of 100. To find X% of Y, divide the percentage by 100 and multiply by the value: (X ÷ 100) × Y. For example, 20% of 500 is (20 ÷ 100) × 500 = 100. This percentage calculator runs that formula the instant you type, and covers five more everyday calculations so you rarely need to reach for a formula sheet.

The six modes explained

X% of Y — the core operation above. Enter a percentage and a base value to get the portion. Useful for VAT, sales tax, deposits, and interest estimates.

X is what % of Y — turns two numbers into a percentage: (X ÷ Y) × 100. If 50 of 200 students passed, that is (50 ÷ 200) × 100 = 25%. Handy for test scores, survey shares, and progress tracking.

Percentage change — measures growth or decline between an original and a new value: ((new − old) ÷ old) × 100. Going from 100 to 150 is a +50% increase; from 150 to 100 is a −33.33% decrease. Note that percentage increase and the reverse decrease are not symmetric, because the base changes.

Discount — shows the final price and the amount saved after a percent off. A 25% discount on 1,000 gives 1,000 − 250 = 750, saving 250. Ideal for sales, clearance, and coupon stacking.

Tip split — adds a tip percentage to a bill and divides the total across a group. A 10% tip on 5,000 makes the total 5,500; split three ways that is about 1,833 each.

Margin / markup — the two most confused business percentages. Margin is profit as a share of the selling price: ((sell − cost) ÷ sell) × 100. Markup is profit as a share of the cost: ((sell − cost) ÷ cost) × 100. A cost of 600 sold for 1,000 gives a 40% margin but a 66.67% markup — the same profit, two different reference points.

Common percentage mistakes to avoid

Reversing a percentage change does not return the original: a value that rises 50% must then fall 33.3% (not 50%) to come back. A 20% discount followed by a further 10% off is not 30% off — the second cut applies to the already-reduced price. And when comparing prices or margins across markets, always keep the reference base consistent. Every result on this page shows the underlying figures so you can check the reasoning at a glance.

What is the difference between margin and markup?
Margin measures profit as a share of the selling price, while markup measures the same profit as a share of the cost price. A product costing 600 and sold for 1,000 has a 40% margin but a 66.67% markup. The Margin mode shows both figures side by side.

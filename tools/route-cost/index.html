Frequently Asked Questions

Is road or rail cheaper for African freight?
For most African routes, road freight is actually more cost-competitive than rail due to poor rail infrastructure, limited networks, and slow transit times. However, for bulk commodities (minerals, agricultural produce) over long distances, rail is cheaper per tonne-km. The SGR (Standard Gauge Railway) in Kenya offers competitive rates between Mombasa and Nairobi. South Africa has the most developed rail freight network in Africa.
How much do border crossings add to African freight costs?
Each border crossing in Africa can add 1-3 days transit time and $50-300 in direct costs (agent fees, border fees, documentation). Unofficial payments and congestion can increase this further. The Beit Bridge (Zimbabwe-South Africa) and Nakonde/Tunduma (Zambia-Tanzania) borders are notorious for delays. The Northern Corridor (Mombasa to East/Central Africa) and Southern African Development Community (SADC) single border-stop initiative have reduced some delays.
What is the cost per tonne-km for road freight in Africa?
Road freight in Africa costs approximately $0.05-0.15/tonne-km depending on route, vehicle utilization, and country. This compares to $0.02-0.05 in Europe and $0.03-0.07 in the US. The higher African cost reflects poor infrastructure, security risks, border delays, vehicle maintenance in difficult conditions, and lower load utilization due to limited return freight options.

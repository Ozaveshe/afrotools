CPS Overview

Nigeria's Contributory Pension Scheme (CPS), governed by the Pension Reform Act 2014, requires:

Employee: Minimum 8% of monthly emoluments (basic + housing + transport)

Employer: Minimum 10% of employee's monthly emoluments

Total: Minimum 18% goes into your Retirement Savings Account (RSA)

Contributions are managed by licensed Pension Fund Administrators (PFAs) and held by Pension Fund Custodians (PFCs).

PFA Options

  • ARM Pension Managers
  • Stanbic IBTC Pension
  • Leadway Pensure
  • AIICO Pension
  • Access Pension (formerly First Guarantee)
  • Premium Pension
  • Trustfund Pensions

Withdrawal Rules

  • Retirement: Age 50+ or per employment contract
  • 25% lump sum withdrawal on retirement
  • Remaining 75% as programmed withdrawal or annuity
  • Voluntary contribution: accessible after 2 years

Understanding Nigeria's Contributory Pension Scheme

Nigeria's pension system underwent a fundamental transformation with the Pension Reform Act 2004 (updated in 2014), which introduced the Contributory Pension Scheme (CPS). This replaced the old Defined Benefits Scheme that was plagued by underfunding and non-payment. Under CPS, both employer and employee make mandatory contributions into individual Retirement Savings Accounts (RSAs) managed by licensed Pension Fund Administrators (PFAs).

The minimum contribution rates are 8% from the employee and 10% from the employer, based on total monthly emoluments. "Monthly emoluments" for pension purposes means the sum of basic salary, housing allowance, and transport allowance — not the entire gross salary. Employers may choose to contribute more than the minimum 10%, and employees can make additional voluntary contributions (AVCs) above the mandatory 8%.

Your pension contributions are invested by your PFA in a portfolio of government securities, corporate bonds, equities, real estate, and other approved investment vehicles. The National Pension Commission (PenCom) regulates the investment guidelines, setting maximum exposure limits for each asset class. Historical returns have averaged 8-12% per annum, though this varies by PFA and market conditions.

Upon retirement (at age 50 or as specified in your employment contract), you can withdraw a lump sum of up to 25% of your RSA balance. The remaining 75% is used to fund either a programmed withdrawal (regular payments from your RSA until it's exhausted) or an annuity (purchased from a life insurance company for guaranteed lifetime payments). The choice between these two options depends on your risk appetite and life expectancy expectations.

Voluntary contributions offer tax advantages — they're deductible from taxable income (within limits) and the investment returns are tax-free. You can access voluntary contributions after 2 years, making them a flexible savings vehicle. Many financial advisors recommend maximizing voluntary contributions as part of a comprehensive retirement planning strategy.

Frequently Asked Questions

What salary components are used for pension?

Pension contributions are calculated on "monthly emoluments" which consists of basic salary + housing allowance + transport allowance. Other allowances like meal, utility, or entertainment are typically not included in the pension base, though employers may choose to include them.

Can I change my PFA?

Yes. Under the PRA 2014, you can transfer your RSA from one PFA to another once a year. Submit a transfer request to your new PFA, and the process takes about 5 working days. Compare PFA performance on PenCom's website before switching.

Is pension contribution tax-deductible?

Yes. Employee pension contributions (up to the statutory 8%) are fully deductible from taxable income under the Personal Income Tax Act. Voluntary contributions above 8% may also qualify for tax relief up to a limit of one-third of your total annual income.

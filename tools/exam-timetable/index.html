How it works — methodology & sources

Updated for 2026. Here is exactly how your timetable is generated, the assumptions behind it, and what to verify before you rely on it.

Methodology & assumptions
The timetable is built entirely in your browser. First we work out how many study blocks fit each day with the formula slots = min(ceil(study hours ÷ 1.5), 8), so each block represents roughly 1.5 hours; weekends are given about half as many blocks plus a rest/review slot, and a break is inserted mid-day. Your subjects are then assigned to those blocks in a round-robin cycle across the chosen number of weeks, after being sorted by the study style you pick — balanced keeps your order, weak-first sorts by priority (highest first), and exam-order sorts by priority ascending. When you set a start date and a target exam date, the duration is derived as ceil(days between ÷ 7) weeks, capped between 1 and 24. The calendar (.ics) export maps each block to a fixed clock time (e.g. Morning 1 = 08:00–09:15). The tool assumes an even distribution of subjects rather than any exam-specific weighting.
Limitations & what to verify
This is a study-planning tool and the schedule it produces is a simple even-distribution heuristic, not personalised pedagogy or an official examination timetable — it is informational only and does not replace guidance from your teacher, school, or exam board. Priorities influence only the ordering of subjects, not the exact number of hours each receives. Before you print, share, or commit to travel, verify the official paper dates, venue, entry details and any calculator or stationery rules with the relevant authority (WAEC, KNEC, ECZ, UNEB, NECTA, ZIMSEC, or the South African DBE). Saved plans live only in this browser's localStorage.

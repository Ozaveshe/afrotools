How to use it well

Once a few schools are on your shortlist, compare the tradeoffs: which one is cheapest, which one looks strongest for research, and which one keeps scholarship options open.

How it works — methodology & sources

Updated for 2026. Here is exactly how the shortlist and fit signal are produced, the assumptions behind them, and what to verify before you rely on them.

Methodology & assumptions
The table is a curated editorial dataset of leading African universities ordered by an overall reputation rank, with each row carrying country, public/private type, student numbers, annual fee bands (indicative USD), and a scholarship flag. The Fit score is a transparent heuristic, not an admission probability: we combine affordability posture (your chosen fee range against the school's fee band), overlap with your saved study fields, saved destination countries, the scholarship flag, and relative ranking strength into a single planning signal. Our assumptions: the rank reflects broad reputation and scale rather than programme-level quality; fee bands are annual estimates that vary by faculty and nationality; and the fit score does not know programme cut-offs, department capacity, or official admissions criteria.
Limitations & what to verify
These are indicative estimates only, not guarantees of admission or outcomes. Rankings and fit scores are planning aids, not admission odds; fees, deadlines, and scholarship availability change frequently, and career or salary outcomes vary widely. This tool is educational and does not replace an admissions adviser. Verify programme-specific entry requirements, accreditation, current tuition and living costs, and application deadlines with each institution's official admissions office before applying.
Sources & references
Based on each university's official prospectus and fee pages plus public university directories and reference rankings such as Times Higher Education ↗ and QS World University Rankings ↗. Rank order and fee bands are editorial estimates cross-checked against these references, not official positions. Last verified 2026.
FAQ

Frequently asked questions

Is the fit score an admission probability?

No. The score is a heuristic planning signal. It reflects affordability, destination alignment, programme overlap, scholarship flags, and ranking strength. It does not know programme-level cut-offs, department capacity, or official admissions criteria.

What makes a saved school more useful now?

Saved schools now feed a compare rail instead of just changing a button label. AfroTools can show which saved school is cheapest, which one anchors research strength, which ones keep scholarship options open, and which schools align with your saved destinations or study focus.

Should I save schools before I know my full budget?

Yes. Save the schools that still feel viable, then use the shortlist rail and Study Abroad Cost to pressure-test the wider plan. A shortlist is more useful when it is imperfect but real than when it never gets built.

How does this connect to Education Hub?

Every saved school becomes a student object in Education Hub. That means you can see universities beside scholarship shortlists, destinations, cost signals, deadlines, and next-best actions instead of losing your research in one standalone tool.

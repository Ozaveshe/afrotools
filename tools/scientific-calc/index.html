How it works — methodology & sources

Updated for 2026. Here is exactly how each result is produced, the assumptions behind it, and what to verify before you rely on it.

Methodology & assumptions
Every calculation runs locally in your browser — nothing is sent to a server. When you press =, the tool rewrites your typed expression into safe JavaScript math: pi and e become the constants Math.PI (3.14159…) and Math.E (2.71828…), the caret ^ becomes exponentiation, and n! is expanded by a factorial loop (defined for whole numbers 0–170; larger values overflow to Infinity). Trigonometric functions respect the selected angle mode: in DEG mode we convert your input with the formula angle × π / 180 before calling sin/cos/tan, and inverse functions (asin, acos, atan) convert their result back to degrees; in RAD mode the raw radian value is used. log maps to base-10 logarithm and ln to the natural logarithm. Unclosed parentheses are auto-closed, the transformed string is validated against an allow-list of math tokens, and only then is it evaluated. Results are formatted to 10 significant figures.
Limitations & what to verify
This is a general-purpose calculator for informational and study use, not a certified instrument. Because it uses standard double-precision (IEEE 754) floating-point arithmetic, some results carry tiny rounding artefacts and are shown to about 10 significant figures — treat displayed digits beyond your data's precision with caution. The single most common source of a "wrong" trig answer is the DEG/RAD switch, so confirm the angle mode before trusting a result. For school, exam, or engineering work, double-check the required rounding, significant figures, and angle mode, and re-key any critical value to confirm it. History exists only in this browser session unless you save or export it.

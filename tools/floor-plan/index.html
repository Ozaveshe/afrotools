How Much Does It Cost to Build in Africa?

Building costs vary enormously across African cities, driven by material prices, labour rates, land regulations, and finish quality. In Lagos, a standard-quality 3-bedroom bungalow costs approximately ₦25-45 million (2024 prices). In Nairobi, a similar build runs KSh 5-10 million. In Johannesburg, expect R800,000-1.5 million. This estimator gives you a realistic baseline for budgeting your project.

Understanding Cost per Square Metre

The construction industry prices buildings by cost per square metre of gross floor area. Economy finishes (basic tiles, simple paint, standard fixtures) cost significantly less than premium or luxury finishes (imported tiles, designer fittings, smart home systems). The cost per m² also varies by building type — bungalows are cheaper per m² than duplexes because duplexes require structural columns, suspended slabs, and staircases.

What's Included in the Estimate

This estimate covers: site preparation and foundations, structural frame (columns, beams, slabs where applicable), walls and blockwork, roofing, doors and windows, floor and wall finishes, plumbing and drainage, electrical installation, painting, external works, professional fees (architect, structural engineer, quantity surveyor), and a 10% contingency. It does not include land purchase, demolition of existing structures, or furniture and appliances.

Common Budget Mistakes

The biggest mistake is underestimating finishes — tiling, painting, kitchen fittings, and bathroom fixtures often cost more than the structural work. Another common error is forgetting professional fees (typically 10-15% of construction cost) and external works (gates, fencing, paving, landscaping). Always add at least 10% contingency for unexpected costs, material price increases, and design changes during construction.

Tips for Cost Control

Get a proper BOQ before construction starts — this prevents disputes and cost overruns. Buy materials in bulk where possible. Use local materials (sandcrete blocks, local timber) instead of imported alternatives. Phase your construction if budget is tight — complete the structure and roof first, then finish rooms gradually. Monitor material deliveries and keep daily site records.

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate is this estimate?
This tool provides a ±20% budget estimate based on average construction costs in each city. For a precise cost, you need a detailed Bill of Quantities (BOQ) prepared by a quantity surveyor based on your architectural drawings. Use our BOQ Builder for that.
Does this include land cost?
No. This estimates construction cost only. Land prices vary dramatically even within the same city. In Lagos, land in Lekki costs 5-10× more than in Ikorodu. Add your actual land purchase cost separately.
What's the difference between economy and luxury?
Economy uses basic materials (local tiles, simple paint, wooden doors). Standard uses mid-range materials. Premium uses imported tiles, aluminium windows, and good fittings. Luxury includes top-grade everything — imported marble, smart home systems, designer fixtures, and premium landscaping.
How long does construction take?
A standard 3-bedroom bungalow takes 4-8 months with consistent funding and good weather. A duplex takes 8-14 months. These timelines assume no significant delays from rain, material shortages, or funding gaps — which in practice add 30-50% to the timeline across Africa.
Why are building costs different across cities?
Material transport costs, labour rates, local regulations, and material availability drive price differences. Coastal cities like Lagos and Mombasa have higher transport costs for inland materials (granite, sand). Cities with booming construction (Abuja, Nairobi) have higher labour rates due to demand.
Should I budget for professional fees?
Yes — always. Architect fees (5-8%), structural engineer (2-3%), quantity surveyor (2-3%), and project management (3-5%) are standard. This tool includes a 12% allocation for professional fees, but actual fees vary by project complexity and negotiation.

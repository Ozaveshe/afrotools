Fuel Market Reading Notes

Reusable context for interpreting fuel-price snapshots without treating them as filing notices, quotes, or regulator announcements.

Methodology

How AfroFuel labels each fuel row before you use it for comparison or generator budgeting.

Price Type
Rows show the latest available retail fuel-price snapshot for petrol, diesel, and LPG where the data supports it. AfroFuel does not mark a row as an official regulator price unless the row is explicitly official verified and includes the exact official source used for that price.
Currency
Local-price columns use the country currency shown beside the row. The USD column is a comparable converted snapshot and should not replace the local-currency price for budgeting.
Unit
Petrol and diesel are shown per litre. LPG is shown per kilogram. Generator-cost shortcuts use litre-based petrol or diesel inputs only.
Date And Source
Each country row shows a source label and row date when the snapshot provides them. If row-level dates are missing, the page uses the snapshot date and labels the row as lower-confidence.

Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about fuel prices in Africa.

AfroFuel labels each page load as an API snapshot, static snapshot, stale snapshot, or unavailable fallback. Static rows may come from third-party market snapshots or reviewed internal data; we do not treat a row as an official regulator price unless the row is explicitly official verified.
Fuel price differences across Africa are driven by several key factors. Government subsidies play the largest role — oil-producing nations like Libya, Algeria, and Angola often subsidise fuel heavily, keeping prices artificially low. Tax structures vary significantly: some countries levy over 40% in taxes and levies on fuel, while others keep taxes minimal. Refining capacity matters too; countries that refine domestically (South Africa, Egypt) avoid import premiums. Landlocked countries like Chad, Central African Republic, and Niger face higher logistics costs for transporting fuel from coastal ports. Exchange rate fluctuations also play a critical role, especially in countries with volatile currencies like Nigeria, Zimbabwe, and Sudan.
Use the current table for this answer instead of relying on a fixed claim. Subsidised oil-producing markets often appear cheaper, while landlocked, island, or import-dependent markets can be higher. AfroFuel shows the cheapest and highest countries in the command desk for the active snapshot and fuel type.
The cost of running a generator in Nigeria depends on generator size, fuel consumption rate, hours of operation, and the selected fuel snapshot. A typical 5 KVA generator consumes approximately 1.5 litres per hour. Running it for 8 hours daily over 26 working days would consume about 312 litres of petrol per month. Use the generator cost calculator above with the visible source state and date before making budget decisions.
PMS (Premium Motor Spirit) is the standard petrol used in cars, motorcycles, and small generators. It is the most widely consumed fuel type across Africa. AGO (Automotive Gas Oil) is diesel fuel, primarily used in trucks, buses, heavy machinery, and large industrial generators. AGO is typically more expensive than PMS but offers better energy density. LPG (Liquefied Petroleum Gas) is cooking gas, increasingly used as a cleaner alternative to charcoal and firewood. LPG pricing is usually quoted per kilogram rather than per litre, and its adoption is being promoted across Africa as part of clean energy transitions.
Fuel pricing rules vary by country. Some markets use administered or capped prices, some use periodic review windows, and others are more market-driven. AfroFuel's regulated badge is a plain-language context label, not proof that the row came from an official regulator. Treat a country row as official regulator data only when an official source link is displayed.
LPG pricing in Africa is influenced by global propane and butane prices, local distribution infrastructure, cylinder exchange costs, and government policies. South Africa and Nigeria have relatively affordable LPG due to domestic production and government pricing interventions. East African nations like Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda tend to have higher LPG costs, partly due to import dependency. LPG is sold in various cylinder sizes (3kg, 6kg, 12.5kg, and 50kg being most common), and per-kilogram prices often decrease with larger cylinder sizes. Many African governments are promoting LPG adoption through tax exemptions and distribution programs to reduce deforestation caused by charcoal and firewood use.

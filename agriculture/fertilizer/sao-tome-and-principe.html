💡 Why Soil Testing Matters
Why should I test my soil?

Soil testing reveals exactly what nutrients your soil already has. Without it, you might over-apply (wasting money) or under-apply (limiting yields).

When NOT to apply fertilizer

Avoid applying on waterlogged soil, during heavy rain, or when crops are drought-stressed. Do not apply urea on hot, dry surfaces.

Fertilizer safety tips

Store in dry, cool places away from children and animals. Wear gloves. Never mix different fertilizers unless recommended.

Fertilizer Use in Sao Tome and Principe

Sao Tome and Principe is a tiny two-island nation in the Gulf of Guinea, with an economy historically built on cocoa production. Agriculture employs about 15% of the population, with cocoa, coffee, coconut, palm oil, bananas, and food crops (maize, cassava, taro, breadfruit) as the main products. Fertilizer consumption is negligible, estimated at under 500 tonnes annually. The volcanic soils are naturally fertile in many areas, but continuous cocoa cultivation over more than a century has depleted nutrients in the traditional plantation zones. All fertilizer is imported, with supply chains passing through Lisbon or regional African ports. The country's small scale and remoteness make fertilizer supply logistics challenging and costly. The government is focused on rehabilitating the cocoa sector and diversifying into organic and fair-trade certified production.

Common Fertilizer Types

NPK 12-12-17 and 15-15-15 are the most commonly available fertilizers in Sao Tome and Principe, used for cocoa and food crops. Urea is applied in small quantities to cocoa and vegetable gardens. For the cocoa sector, which is increasingly targeting organic and specialty markets, organic soil amendments are emphasized over mineral fertilizer. Cocoa pod husks and pruning residues are composted and returned to the plantations as organic mulch and nutrient sources. Rock phosphate is occasionally available through development projects. The naturally fertile volcanic soils reduce the urgency of mineral fertilization compared to many mainland African countries, but aging cocoa trees on depleted soils do benefit significantly from targeted nutrient application. Shade management in cocoa agroforestry systems, using nitrogen-fixing shade trees like Erythrina and Gliricidia, provides a biological source of nitrogen.

Subsidy Programs & Access

Sao Tome and Principe does not maintain a formal fertilizer subsidy program given the tiny scale of its agricultural sector. Input support is provided through agricultural development projects funded by international partners including Portugal, the EU, IFAD, and FAO. The Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries, and Rural Development coordinates input distribution when project resources are available. The PAPAFPA (Project to Support Small-Scale Agriculture and Fisheries) has included input provision for smallholder cocoa and food crop farmers. Distribution occurs through local agricultural offices and community associations. The cocoa sector rehabilitation programs, supported by various bilateral and multilateral donors, include fertilizer and improved planting material provision to cocoa farmers. Given the small size of the country (approximately 1,000 square kilometers across two islands), distribution logistics are manageable, though the lack of a steady commercial supply remains a constraint for farmers wanting to purchase fertilizer outside of project distributions.

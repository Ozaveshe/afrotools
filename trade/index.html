Frequently Asked Questions

Trade & Import — Common Questions

What is the AfCFTA and how does it affect import duties? ▼

The African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA) is a single market for goods and services across Africa's 54 countries. Under Phase I (2021–2026), tariffs on 90% of goods are being eliminated progressively. Our AfCFTA Tariff Tracker lets you check the current preferential rate for any product between any two African countries.

What is the ECOWAS Common External Tariff (CET)?▼

The ECOWAS CET is a unified tariff system applied to goods imported from outside the 15-member ECOWAS bloc. It has 5 bands: 0% (essential goods), 5% (raw materials), 10% (intermediate goods), 20% (consumer goods), and 35% (sensitive products like rice, poultry). Each country may add levies on top — Nigeria adds VAT, CISS and a surcharge, making the effective rate much higher.

What trade finance option is best for African importers?▼

It depends on your situation. For first-time suppliers, a Sight Letter of Credit (LC) provides maximum protection. For established relationships, Cash Against Documents (CAD) is cheaper. For small orders under $5,000, T/T Advance is simplest despite the risk. Use our Trade Finance Comparator to get a personalised recommendation based on your specific trade scenario.

How do SADC Rules of Origin work?▼

SADC rules of origin determine whether a product can benefit from preferential (lower) tariff rates when traded between SADC member states. The general rule is that at least 35% of the ex-works price must be added within SADC. Textiles have stricter "fabric forward" rules, while machinery requires either a change of tariff heading or max 60% non-SADC content. Use our SADC ROO Checker to test your specific product.

What is PAPSS and why does it matter for African trade?▼

PAPSS (Pan-African Payment & Settlement System) is a payment infrastructure built by Afreximbank to enable intra-African transactions in local currencies, eliminating the need to convert to USD for Africa-to-Africa payments. This removes 2–4% FX costs per transaction. It is central to making AfCFTA trade commercially viable and is being progressively rolled out through African central banks.

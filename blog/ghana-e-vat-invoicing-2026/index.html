Ghana E-VAT is now one of the most important compliance topics for VAT registered businesses because it connects the tax invoice, the sales receipt, the customer evidence trail and the monthly VAT return. It is easy to describe it as software, but that is too narrow. For a finance team, E-VAT is the control that decides whether a sale has a GRA-certified invoice trail before the VAT return is prepared.

Use it with the Ghana VAT calculator, the Ghana VAT guide, and the Ghana withholding tax guide when you need the surrounding tax treatment.

This article is intentionally narrower than a general VAT guide. It focuses on invoicing readiness: who needs to pay attention, which setup route GRA describes, what a valid E-VAT receipt should show, how offline use works, and how to build a month-end check that does not confuse E-VAT with VAT registration, withholding VAT or ordinary WHT.

The operating point is simple. E-VAT makes the invoice trail more visible and easier to check, but it does not remove the monthly filing obligation, the need to classify supplies correctly, or the need to reconcile output tax, input tax, withholding VAT credits and payments before the return is submitted.

Rate And Reform Context For 2026

Ghana's VAT structure changed materially from January 1, 2026 under the Value Added Tax Act, 2025 (Act 1151). GRA's notice to VAT registered taxpayers says the COVID-19 Health Recovery Levy has been abolished. The same notice says VAT remains at 15 percent while the National Health Insurance Levy and Ghana Education Trust Fund levy stay at 2.5 percent each, bringing the total effective VAT rate to 20 percent.

That rate context matters for E-VAT because invoice software and templates can become stale. A system that still adds a 1 percent COVID levy, uses an old flat-rate setup as the default, or separates NHIL and GETFund in a way that does not match 2026 guidance will create wrong customer documents even if the business knows the headline VAT rate.

GRA also says the threshold for VAT registration for businesses dealing in goods increased from GHS 200,000 to GHS 750,000. That threshold point belongs to registration, not to E-VAT installation by itself, but the two questions often arrive together when a business grows. First confirm whether the business is registered or must register. Then confirm whether the invoicing path is ready to issue GRA-certified electronic VAT receipts.

Setup Paths And Installation

GRA's E-VAT page describes two forms of the system. The first is an all-round e-invoicing system installed free of charge for VAT registered businesses that do not already have one. The second is a version that can be integrated with the existing invoicing system of VAT registered businesses. GRA says either option will be installed by GRA experts based on the beneficiary business's choice.

The 2024 certified invoicing system guideline adds more operational detail. For taxpayers with their own enterprise resource planning or point-of-sale systems, the existing system can be integrated with GRA's invoicing system and approved for issuing Commissioner-General certified electronic VAT invoices. For taxpayers issuing manual VAT invoices, GRA describes free invoicing software hosted in a cloud environment, with desktop, mobile, online and portal options.

The onboarding procedure in the same guideline is useful because it shows that E-VAT setup is not just a download. It says a taxpayer may be selected, may request onboarding, or may be onboarded at the point of VAT registration. A relationship manager assists the taxpayer, an onboarding form is completed, GRA determines whether the taxpayer needs integration or free invoicing software, testing is performed, and GRA signs off before the taxpayer is scheduled to go live.

GRA's public E-VAT page says a business should write to its Taxpayer Service Centre requesting the E-VAT system and that the TSC will guide the process. It lists a desktop or laptop computer, internet access and a web browser as installation requirements. Those are minimum setup points, not the full control list. Finance teams should also check user permissions, invoice numbering, branch or till mapping, VAT code setup, refund and credit-note workflow, and who is responsible for resolving failed transmissions.

What A Valid E-VAT Receipt Should Show

GRA's E-VAT page lists visible security features that customers can expect on an E-VAT receipt. These include the Commissioner-General's signature, a QR code for verification, a receipt signature that cannot be altered, the exact date and time, a serial number, a receipt number, and a machine registration code.

Those details should become part of buyer-side checks. A customer that needs input tax support should not only ask for a PDF invoice. It should confirm that the document carries the expected GRA-certified receipt features, that the supplier name and TIN match the contract or purchase order, that the VAT and levy treatment matches the supply, and that the receipt is retained with the accounting entry.

For sellers, receipt quality is commercial as well as tax-related. If buyers cannot verify a VAT receipt or cannot connect it to a purchase order, payment can slow down. Larger customers may also require cleaner E-VAT evidence before accepting input tax or expense support. That makes E-VAT readiness part of customer service, not just compliance.

Invoice teams should also distinguish between a receipt feature and a tax decision. A QR code and signature help prove the receipt was generated through the electronic system. They do not prove that the supply was correctly classified as taxable, exempt, zero-rated or outside scope. Classification still needs ordinary VAT review, especially for mixed supplies, imported services, non-life insurance, immovable property, exports and transactions involving withholding VAT agents.

Offline And Downtime Controls

GRA's E-VAT page says users can generate electronic receipts online or offline for up to 24 hours. That is a practical feature for businesses that cannot stop selling because a connection drops, but it should not become a loose workaround. Offline receipts still need to be reconciled once connectivity returns.

The certified invoicing system guideline explains downtime procedures. If the taxpayer's online E-invoicing software is offline, the taxpayer should immediately notify GRA through the support email listed in the guideline. GRA then acknowledges the complaint and can issue the signature key automatically to the taxpayer's preferred contact to avoid business disruption. The guideline also says the taxpayer must update the Commissioner-General's system with all transactions when the system was offline.

For ERP or point-of-sale downtime, the guideline says the taxpayer should immediately notify GRA and GRA should verify the claim. For system upgrades, hardware upgrades, software upgrades or antivirus installation, the taxpayer should notify GRA 24 hours before the upgrade through the official reporting platform. Those details matter because system changes can break integrations, invoice numbering or transmission logs if nobody owns the change window.

A sensible internal control is to keep an E-VAT downtime register. Record the date, start time, end time, reason, GRA notification reference, signature-key handling, offline invoices issued, transactions uploaded later, and the staff member who reconciled the batch. That register should be reviewed before the monthly VAT return is filed.

Monthly Finance Workflow

E-VAT should sit inside the monthly VAT close, not beside it. GRA's VAT page says VAT, NHIL and GETFund returns and payments are due by the last working day of the month immediately following the month to which the return relates. That deadline means an invoice problem in week one can become a filing problem by month-end if it is not caught early.

Start the close with a sales register exported from the invoicing system. Match total taxable sales, exempt sales, zero-rated sales and credit notes to the accounting ledger. Then reconcile the E-VAT issued receipts to bank receipts, point-of-sale reports, delivery notes and customer statements. Differences should be explained before the return is drafted, not after the return is submitted.

Next, review input tax support. Act 1151 reforms changed how NHIL and GETFund levies are treated for input tax credit claims, according to GRA's taxpayer notice. That makes old input-tax schedules risky. Finance teams should update calculation templates and accounting mappings so the current 20 percent effective charge is not treated like the older levy structure.

Then check withholding VAT. If a customer is an appointed withholding VAT agent, GRA's VAT withholding guidance says a separate credit-certificate workflow applies. Do not treat withholding VAT as a missing receipt or as ordinary WHT. A single customer invoice can require E-VAT receipt support, normal VAT return treatment, and withholding VAT credit evidence.

Finally, keep proof. Save the E-VAT export, VAT working paper, input tax schedule, credit notes, withholding VAT certificates, downtime register and return acknowledgement in one month folder. The point is not to collect documents for their own sake. It is to make the next review faster and to protect the business if a customer, auditor or GRA officer asks how the return was built.

Stale Ghana E-VAT Claims To Remove

  • Stale claim one: E-VAT is a new tax. GRA says it is not a new tax. It is an electronic invoicing solution for VAT-able transactions.
  • Stale claim two: A manual PDF invoice is enough for VAT registered sales. The E-VAT workflow is about GRA-certified electronic invoice evidence, not only the visual invoice layout.
  • Stale claim three: Ghana VAT still includes the 1 percent COVID levy. GRA's 2026 notice says the COVID-19 Health Recovery Levy has been abolished.
  • Stale claim four: The old goods registration threshold is still the general current line. GRA's 2026 notice says the threshold for businesses dealing in goods increased to GHS 750,000.
  • Stale claim five: Offline receipt generation means transactions can stay outside the GRA system. GRA's guideline says transactions from offline periods must be updated in the Commissioner-General's system.
  • Stale claim six: E-VAT removes the monthly return. It does not. The VAT, NHIL and GETFund return and payment deadline still applies.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Ghana E-VAT a new tax?

No. GRA describes E-VAT as an electronic system for issuing VAT receipts and says it is not a new tax. It is an invoicing and record-keeping system for VAT-able transactions.

What rate should Ghana VAT invoices use in 2026?

GRA's 2026 VAT reform notice says VAT remains 15 percent and NHIL and GETFund remain 2.5 percent each, creating a 20 percent effective charge on the same base value.

What E-VAT setup options does GRA describe?

GRA describes an all-round e-invoicing system installed free for VAT registered businesses without an invoicing system and an integration option for businesses with an existing invoicing system.

What should appear on a Ghana E-VAT receipt?

GRA lists security features including the Commissioner-General's signature, a QR code, a receipt signature, exact date and time, serial number, receipt number and machine registration code.

Can Ghana E-VAT receipts be generated offline?

GRA's E-VAT page says users can generate electronic receipts online or offline for up to 24 hours. The administrative guideline also says taxpayers must update the Commissioner-General's system with transactions when the taxpayer system was offline.

Does E-VAT replace monthly VAT filing?

No. E-VAT improves the invoice trail. GRA's VAT page still says VAT, NHIL and GETFund returns and payments are due by the last working day of the month after the return month.

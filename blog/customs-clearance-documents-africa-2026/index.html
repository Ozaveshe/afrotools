African importers often lose money after the purchase decision, not at the purchase decision. The supplier invoice is paid, the container or parcel is moving, and then clearance slows because one document is missing, the declared description is vague, the permit path was not checked, or the clearing agent cannot reconcile the invoice, packing list and transport document. At that point the cost problem is no longer only customs duty. It becomes storage, demurrage, amendment, inspection, exchange-rate movement and delayed resale.

This guide focuses on the document and timing layer of import clearance. It is not an official customs ruling, tariff schedule or port tariff. Use it to prepare a clean packet before cargo arrives, then confirm the exact declaration, payment and release path with the destination customs authority, port system, regulator, bank, carrier, forwarder or licensed customs agent.

Rates, due-entry windows, port tariffs, free-time rules and regulator procedures can change, so verify the current official page before shipping or paying.

Prepare the packet before the goods arrive. At minimum, most import workflows need a commercial invoice, packing list, transport document, importer tax identifier, customs declaration details, freight or insurance evidence where relevant, and permits or standards certificates for regulated goods. Why Documents Drive Clearance Timing

Customs clearance is a control process. Authorities check who is importing, what is being imported, how the goods are described, how value is supported, whether the HS code or tariff heading is credible, whether restricted goods have permits, whether duties and taxes have been assessed and paid, and whether another agency needs to inspect the cargo. The port, terminal, carrier and warehouse also need release instructions before cargo can leave the controlled area.

That is why a customs problem can start with a commercial document. If the invoice says one product name and the packing list says another, the agent may need a correction before entry. If the bill of lading shows a consignee name that does not match the tax or business identity, release can slow. If the product is regulated but the certificate of conformity, health permit, standards approval or import license was not prepared, the declaration may move into inspection or query status. If the customs value needs freight and insurance support and those figures are missing, the estimate becomes fragile.

For small businesses, the safest habit is to treat the import file as a working packet, not a collection of attachments. The invoice, packing list, transport document, permit, certificate, payment proof, HS code note and landed-cost estimate should tell one consistent story. If a clearing agent, bank officer, port terminal, customs officer or standards regulator reads the file, the product identity and value basis should be easy to trace.

Country Checks That Change the Packet

Nigeria: Nigeria Customs Service public guidance lists documents such as bill of lading, commercial invoice, exit note, Form M entry declaration, packing list, single goods declaration and product certificate for import clearance. It also describes PAAR as a mandatory pre-arrival assessment document for imports. Nigeria Ports Authority material points importers through Form M, final invoice, bill of lading or airway bill, packing list, SONCAP where applicable and PAAR issuance. The planning lesson is to settle Form M, product certificate and PAAR questions before the shipment is near Lagos, Onne or another port.

Kenya: Kenya Revenue Authority guidance says imported goods are declared through a licensed customs clearing agent and lists documents that can include certificate of conformity, import standards mark where applicable, commercial invoice, pro forma invoice, bill of lading or airway bill, certificate of origin, freight invoice, permit or license for restricted goods, PIN certificate, purchase documents, roadworthiness certificate for motor vehicles, packing list and letter of credit where available. KRA also states that imported goods are generally entered within the EAC legal due-entry period, so timing matters as much as the document list.

Ghana: Ghana Revenue Authority import-procedure guidance says importers should have documents before or at arrival to facilitate clearance, including original waybill or bill of lading, attested invoice, packing list, Import Declaration Form, TIN or GhanaCard PIN and permits from regulators such as FDA or Ghana Standards Authority where the goods require them. GRA says the clearing agent submits the declaration in ICUMS and that processing can move through classification, valuation and approval before payment and risk-level checks. Ghana Ports and Harbours Authority also lists port-process documents such as original bill of lading, certified invoice, IDF, customs classification and valuation report, tax clearance certificate, TIN, delivery order, permits, licenses and validated cargo tracking note.

South Africa: South African Revenue Service import guidance explains that the clearance process checks the goods declaration against documents such as invoice, bill of lading, certificate of origin and permits, then covers examination if necessary and assessment and collection of duty and VAT. SARS also publishes due-entry timeframes: seven days from landing for many goods, fourteen days for loose or break bulk cargo imported by sea, air or rail, and twenty-eight days for goods in a container depot. That makes South Africa a good reminder that document readiness should be measured against statutory timing, not only against the day the importer wants delivery.

Port and terminal layer: Port tariffs are not the same as customs duties. Nigeria Ports Authority lists a tariff schedule with an effective date of March 1, 2025. Kenya Ports Authority's tariff book defines free periods and says the import and transhipment storage free period starts from the day after discharge. Transnet publishes annual tariff books for South African port systems. Treat these as live operational cost sources, not static blog numbers. The carrier, terminal and port tariff can change the cash needed even after customs assessment is settled.

How Document Gaps Become Demurrage Risk

Demurrage and storage are timing penalties in practical form. They often appear when a container, vehicle, parcel or bulk shipment stays under port, terminal, depot or carrier control after the allowed free period or agreed movement window. Customs may be finished but the terminal still needs a delivery order. The declaration may be ready but the bank payment has not posted. The tax bill may be paid but a regulator inspection is pending. The agent may have a release note but the carrier has not released the transport document.

Do not budget delay as one vague line. Split it into five checks:

  1. Declaration readiness: can the agent lodge the entry from the invoice, packing list, transport document, HS code, permits and importer identity?
  2. Payment readiness: is the importer ready to pay duties, taxes, bank charges, port charges, carrier charges and agent fees when the bill is generated?
  3. Inspection readiness: are product certificates, standards approvals, health permits, origin proof and sample requirements already known?
  4. Release readiness: does the importer have the delivery order, original or electronic transport document path, terminal requirements and final pickup plan?
  5. Delivery readiness: is truck booking, warehouse access, offloading, local insurance and inland delivery timing confirmed?

If the estimate becomes commercially fragile after adding three to seven extra days of storage or carrier charges, the import may need stronger document work before payment.

A Practical AfroTools Workflow Before You Ship

Use this sequence when a supplier quote looks attractive but the clearance path is not yet proven:

  1. Write the product description in plain language, then search a planning tariff heading with HS Code Lookup.
  2. Build a draft commercial invoice and packing list with enough detail for a clearing agent to review before payment.
  3. Ask whether the destination country requires IDF, Form M, PAAR, certificate of conformity, import permit, standards approval, health permit, certificate of origin, roadworthiness certificate or another regulator document.
  4. Estimate duty and tax with Import Duty Calculator, then mark uncertain classification, value and permit lines for official confirmation.
  5. Build the full landed cost in Landed Cost Calculator, including freight, insurance, agent fees, port charges, local delivery and delay buffer.
  6. Run the likely release window through Customs Clearance Time Estimator and stress-test free-time exposure in Demurrage Calculator.
  7. Keep the final packet in one folder: invoice, packing list, transport document, permits, classification note, value support, payment proof, agent quote and source links checked on the date of approval.

The purpose is not to remove every risk. It is to stop preventable risks before the cargo leaves the origin country. A clean packet will not guarantee a green customs lane, but it gives the importer, agent, bank, carrier and warehouse fewer reasons to stop and ask basic questions under time pressure.

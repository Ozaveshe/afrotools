A proper Nigeria estimate starts with purchase price, FOB or CIF value, then adds customs assessment, port and terminal costs, clearing agent work, storage risk, inland movement, registration and FX sensitivity.

Nigeria is one of the markets where old blog posts become dangerous fastest. The applicable tariff line, customs valuation method, automotive policy layer, exchange rate and port delay can all change the real landed cost. A buyer comparing a Lagos-cleared import with a local dealer car should model both customs and non-customs costs before deciding.

Start with documents and PAAR

Nigeria Customs public guidance says import clearance can require documents such as bill of lading, commercial invoice, exit note, Form M entry declaration, packing list, single goods declaration and product certificate. It also explains PAAR, the Pre-Arrival Assessment Report, as a mandatory import document that details the goods and value before arrival.

That matters for cars because a late or inconsistent document pack can create a cost problem even when the duty estimate looked fine. If the VIN, invoice, title, bill of lading and Form M details do not line up cleanly, the risk is not only a higher payment. It can also mean delay, storage, extra agent work and a harder release process.

Do not hard-code old rate assumptions

Older Nigeria automotive tariff circulars are still useful for understanding why vehicle classification matters, but they should not be treated as the only current authority for a 2026 purchase. Use them as background, then confirm the live tariff, valuation and prohibition position with Nigeria Customs, the current tariff tool, B'Odogwu or a licensed clearing agent.

This is especially important for age and vehicle-category questions. Public summaries often conflict, and enforcement can depend on the exact category and current policy notice. If the car is near an age boundary or falls outside an ordinary passenger-vehicle profile, get confirmation before payment.

Formula walkthrough

A practical Nigeria planning formula is: landed cost equals customs value plus customs duty and other import taxes, plus port and shipping-line charges, plus clearing and documentation, plus storage buffer, plus inland delivery and registration. Keep a separate line for demurrage or storage risk. Lagos port delays can turn that line from a placeholder into a real cost.

Run at least two estimates. The first should use the expected exchange rate and assessed value. The second should use a weaker naira or higher valuation. If the second estimate makes the import unattractive, treat the purchase as fragile and negotiate before shipping.

What buyers usually miss

  • PAAR and document consistency can matter as much as the duty percentage.
  • CIF valuation can differ from the number in a seller advert.
  • VAT, NAC levy and other policy charges need to be checked for the exact category.
  • Terminal handling, storage, demurrage and clearing agent costs belong in the budget.
  • Age and eligibility should be confirmed with a current official source before purchase.

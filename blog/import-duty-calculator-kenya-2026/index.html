How Kenya Import Duty Works

Importing goods into Kenya is expensive because several charges can apply to the same shipment. Customs duty, Import Declaration Fee (IDF), Railway Development Levy (RDL), excise duty, and VAT can all affect the landed cost before you clear goods at Mombasa port, JKIA, or an inland container depot.

The Kenya Revenue Authority (KRA) administers all import charges through the Integrated Customs Management System (iCMS). Every commercial import requires a customs entry, and the system calculates your total liability based on the HS code (Harmonized System code) assigned to your product.

Source check, June 17, 2026: KRA's current importing page states IDF at 3.5% and Railway Development Levy at 2% on the value of imports. KRA vehicle guidance also lists 25% import duty, excise duty, 16% VAT, IDF, and RDL for motor vehicle imports. Use the final iCMS assessment, customs tariff, and any current gazette notice for your exact HS code.

Here's the formula you need to remember:

Total Landing Cost = CIF + Import Duty + Excise Duty + VAT (16%) + IDF (3.5%) + RDL (2%) + clearing costs

CIF stands for Cost, Insurance, and Freight. It's the value of your goods including the purchase price, shipping insurance, and freight charges to the Kenyan port of entry. This is the base number everything else is calculated from.

IDF and RDL

KRA's current importing page reviewed on June 17, 2026 says Import Declaration Fee is 3.5% and Railway Development Levy is 2% on the value of imports under the Miscellaneous Fees and Levies Act framework.

These are separate from customs duty. A product can have 0% import duty and still attract IDF, RDL, VAT, inspection charges, clearing-agent fees, and storage if documents are delayed. Some goods and importers can have exemptions, so confirm the iCMS assessment rather than relying on a generic calculator alone.

You pay import levies and taxes through the official KRA customs process. Your clearing agent should show the declared customs value, HS code, duty rate, IDF, RDL, VAT, excise duty if any, and supporting documents before you approve payment.

Import Duty Rates by Category

Kenya follows the East African Community (EAC) Common External Tariff. Duty rates range from 0% to 100% depending on what you're importing. The general logic: raw materials come in cheap, finished goods get taxed heavily to protect local manufacturers.

These rates can change through EAC tariff updates, Kenya Finance Acts, legal notices, and customs rulings. Always verify your specific HS code on the KRA customs tariff or through a licensed clearing agent before committing to a shipment. A product you think is 25% might fall under a different classification or special levy.

Excise Duty on Vehicles and Select Goods

Excise duty is a separate charge that hits specific product categories. Vehicles get the worst of it.

Motor vehicles: KRA guidance lists 25% import duty on customs value, excise duty based on the vehicle category, 16% VAT on CIF plus import duty plus excise duty, plus IDF and RDL. Do not use one flat vehicle formula for every car because engine size, fuel type, age, and KRA's Current Retail Selling Price schedule can change the assessment.

Age restriction: Kenya generally restricts importation of used vehicles older than 8 years from the year of first registration, with compliance checked through the vehicle import process. Confirm the current KEBS and KRA rule before buying abroad because the date test is strict.

Other goods attracting excise duty include alcoholic beverages, tobacco products, bottled water, cosmetics, and certain plastic products. The rates vary from 10% to over 100% for items like cigarettes.

VAT on Imports (16%)

Here's where the costs really compound. KRA vehicle guidance calculates VAT at 16% on the CIF value plus import duty plus excise duty. IDF and RDL are separate levies on import value, so keep them visible instead of burying them inside the VAT base.

This compounding effect is what catches first-time importers off guard. On a vehicle example with 25% duty and 20% excise, VAT is charged on a base that has already grown above the CIF value. IDF, RDL, registration costs, inspection costs, storage, and clearing-agent fees then sit outside that simple tax stack.

If you're registered for VAT in Kenya (turnover above KES 5 million), you can claim back the import VAT as input tax against your output VAT on local sales. But you need to be VAT-registered first, and the refund process through KRA can take months.

Electronics are a lower-duty scenario. Zero import duty means the main official tax stack is IDF, RDL, and VAT, before any clearing, inspection, storage, or delivery cost. This laptop example adds about 21.5% to the CIF price before non-tax charges.

The car that cost KES 1.2 million to buy and ship now costs about KES 2.154 million before registration fees, inspection charges, storage, delivery, and the clearing agent's commission. This example assumes the 20% excise case; your actual vehicle assessment can differ under KRA's CRSP and excise rules.

New clothing attracts 25% duty. Used clothing (mitumba) can face even higher rates of 35% or specific duties per kilogram, depending on current government policy. The rates on second-hand clothing have changed several times in recent years as Kenya tries to protect its textile industry.

The Import Process Step by Step

Before your goods arrive in Kenya, there's paperwork to sort out. Here's the typical flow for a commercial import through Mombasa.

  1. Get a KRA PIN if you don't have one. Both individuals and businesses need this.
  2. Hire a licensed clearing agent unless you want to learn the iCMS system yourself. Most importers use agents. Their fee runs between KES 15,000 and KES 50,000 depending on shipment complexity.
  3. Obtain a Pre-Export Verification of Conformity (PVoC) certificate from the current KEBS-appointed inspection route for your export country and product. Some goods are exempt or handled through destination inspection.
  4. Submit the Import Declaration Form (IDF) through iCMS before shipment or upon arrival. Confirm the assessed IDF, RDL, duty, excise, and VAT before payment.
  5. Submit the customs entry once goods arrive. Your clearing agent handles this, attaching the commercial invoice, bill of lading, packing list, and Certificate of Conformity or exemption document where required.
  6. Pay all duties and VAT through the KRA payment portal. You'll get a release order once payment clears.
  7. Clear your goods from the port or inland container depot. Budget 3–7 working days from ship arrival to clearance if documents are in order.

Missing documents are one of the biggest causes of delays. KEBS explains that imported goods may need a Certificate of Conformity through the Pre-Export Verification of Conformity programme or destination inspection if exempted or not covered. Do not ship first and hope to solve standards paperwork at the port.

Kenya VAT is one of those rulesets that looks straightforward until you need to apply it to a real invoice. The headline rate is easy to remember. The operational detail is not. Businesses still get caught by the KSh 5 million registration threshold, by the distinction between zero-rated and exempt supplies, by eTIMS invoicing requirements, and by the difference between ordinary monthly VAT filing and special cases such as imported services or withholding VAT.

Current Kenya VAT Rates

KRA's current VAT page lists two active rates in 2026. The first is the 16 percent general rate, which applies to taxable goods and services that are not specifically zero-rated. The second is the 0 percent zero-rate, which applies to supplies listed in the Second Schedule to the VAT Act, 2013.

The outdated point many Kenya VAT explainers still miss is the petroleum rate. KRA now explicitly notes that the old 8 percent other rate for certain petroleum supplies was deleted by the Finance Act, 2023 with effect from July 1, 2023. So if you still see a 2026 Kenya VAT calculator presenting 8 percent as a live reduced rate, treat that as a stale page, not current KRA guidance.

There is also a third concept that gets confused with the 0 percent rate: exempt supplies. Exempt supplies are not taxable supplies, and KRA says related input tax is not deductible. That makes exempt treatment materially different from zero-rating. A zero-rated seller can still sit inside the VAT system and may recover qualifying input tax. A seller making only exempt supplies usually does not.

Practical rule: 16 percent means taxable. 0 percent means still taxable but at a zero rate. Exempt means outside the taxable supply bucket for output VAT purposes and usually without input tax deduction on related costs.

Who Must Register For VAT

KRA says any person supplying or expecting to supply taxable goods and taxable services with a value of KSh 5 million or more in a year is required to register for VAT. This matters because older Kenya content still repeats a KSh 16 million number that no longer matches the current KRA VAT page.

If your business has not yet reached the threshold, KRA says voluntary registration can be granted subject to conditions. That can matter for businesses that mainly sell to VAT registered customers and want input tax recovery and compliant invoicing from the start.

There is also an important rule for cross-border digital activity. KRA says non-resident persons making supplies in Kenya over the internet, an electronic network, or a digital marketplace must register for VAT whether or not their taxable supplies meet the annual threshold of KES 5 million. That means the threshold is not a universal escape valve for every platform model.

KRA's Turnover Tax page reinforces the same KSh 5 million threshold from a different angle. It notes that a Turnover Tax taxpayer dealing in vatable supplies and reaching KSh 5,000,000 and above is required to register for VAT as well. That cross-check is useful because it reduces the risk of relying on one isolated page.

How Output VAT And Input VAT Work

Kenya VAT works on an input tax versus output tax system. KRA defines input tax as the VAT paid by a registered person on purchases of goods or services for the purpose of the business. Output tax is the VAT charged on sales of taxable goods or services. The tax payable for the period is the difference between the two.

In formula form, KRA states it plainly: Output Tax minus Input Tax equals Tax Payable.

KRA's own published worked example is useful because it shows the mechanics without inventing a made-up case. It starts with a purchase net price of KSh 10,000, VAT of KSh 1,600, then adds a 20 percent profit margin of KSh 2,000 to reach a net sales price of KSh 12,000. Applying 16 percent output VAT creates KSh 1,920 of output tax and a selling price of KSh 13,920. The tax payable becomes KSh 1,920 minus KSh 1,600, which equals KSh 320.

That worked example matters for another reason. It reminds you that VAT is not a tax on turnover in the same way Turnover Tax is. It is designed to tax value added across the chain, with deductible input tax for qualifying business purchases.

KRA also says input tax deduction is valid only within six months after the end of the tax period in which the supply or importation occurred, and only where the registered person has valid documentation to support the claim. That is where eTIMS discipline and record-keeping stop being administrative chores and start affecting real money.

Filing, Payment, eTIMS And Records

KRA's current VAT page states that VAT is due on or before the 20th day of the following month, and that this includes both the return and the payment. Returns are submitted online through iTax. If your business has output VAT for March, the filing and payment deadline is April 20.

KRA also says every VAT registered taxpayer is required to onboard on eTIMS. The same VAT page states that tax invoices should be generated from eTIMS, and KRA's 2023 public notice on electronic tax invoicing reinforced the operational consequence: compliant electronic tax invoices matter for claiming input tax and processing refunds.

KRA's public notice for non-VAT registered persons is a useful extra check for 2026 operations: it reminds all persons carrying on business, including those not registered for VAT, to electronically generate and transmit invoices through eTIMS, and says unsupported business expenditure is not deductible for income tax purposes from January 1, 2024. In other words, eTIMS discipline now matters beyond the VAT return itself.

For day-to-day operations, the cleanest way to think about this is:

  1. Register once your taxable supplies meet or are expected to meet the KSh 5 million threshold, or earlier if KRA approves voluntary registration.
  2. Issue compliant eTIMS invoices for taxable supplies.
  3. Keep proper transaction records in a format that supports output tax, input tax, and invoice validation.
  4. File the monthly VAT return in iTax.
  5. Pay any net VAT due by the 20th day of the following month.

KRA's step-by-step VAT payment instructions are also published on the VAT page itself. They direct taxpayers to log in to iTax, choose the VAT tax head and subhead, select the relevant tax period, register the payment, and then pay using the permitted mode.

Another operational point people miss is the tax point. KRA says VAT becomes due at the earliest of several events, including delivery of goods or performance of services, issuance of the invoice, or receipt of payment in whole or part. That means you should not treat invoice date, payment date, and tax date as automatically identical.

Reverse VAT, Zero-Rating And Withholding VAT

Kenya VAT gets more technical once you move beyond ordinary local sales. One important special case is imported services, often described in practice as reverse VAT. KRA says any importer of an imported service is liable to VAT irrespective of VAT registration status. The liability arises when the service is received, the invoice is received, or payment is made, whichever comes first.

For VAT registered businesses, KRA adds that VAT paid on imported services for use in the taxable business may be deducted as input tax in subsequent VAT returns. That is why reverse VAT is not just a calculation issue. It changes the tax cash flow and the documentation chain.

Another special case is withholding VAT. KRA says the current withholding VAT rate is 2 percent of the value of taxable supplies, effective from November 7, 2019, and that appointed withholding VAT agents remit the amount to the Commissioner on the 20th day of the month following deduction. If your business sells to large appointed agents, this affects the cash you receive and the credits you apply in the return.

Zero-rating is the opposite of withholding VAT in one important way. It can create businesses with very low output tax but meaningful input tax. KRA says excess input tax can be carried forward or refunded in specific cases, including where the excess arises from making zero-rated supplies. That is why exporters and businesses heavily exposed to zero-rated transactions need a tighter documentation process than the average local trader.

Penalties And Common Failure Points

KRA's business penalties page is the most useful current operational table for VAT defaults. It says:

  • Late filing of the VAT return: 5 percent of the tax due or KSh 10,000, whichever is higher.
  • Late payment of VAT tax: 5 percent of the tax due and interest of 1 percent per month.

That means a business can create two separate problems in the same period: it can file late, and it can pay late. The safest workflow is to treat both steps as hard monthly controls, not as one combined finance task done whenever someone remembers.

In practice, the recurring Kenya VAT failure points are usually these:

  • Keeping an outdated threshold or rate in an internal calculator.
  • Confusing zero-rated supplies with exempt supplies.
  • Missing eTIMS compliance and then discovering input tax support is weak.
  • Ignoring imported services because no local invoice was issued.
  • Forgetting that withholding VAT credits affect the net position.
  • Waiting until the 20th without reconciling invoices, credit notes, and tax point timing.

What is a Domiciliary Account?

A domiciliary account (commonly called a "dom account") is a foreign currency current account held at a Nigerian commercial bank. Unlike a standard naira savings or current account, a dom account holds balances denominated in a foreign currency - most commonly the US Dollar (USD), British Pound (GBP), or Euro (EUR). The funds in the account are owned by the account holder and are maintained in the foreign currency; they are not automatically converted to naira.

The Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN) regulates domiciliary accounts under the Foreign Exchange (Monitoring and Miscellaneous Provisions) Act and associated circulars. The CBN has, over the years, issued various directives governing how funds in dom accounts can be deposited, withdrawn, and transferred. Key CBN rules include:

  • Funds can be deposited via cash lodgement, inward transfers, or proceeds from export transactions.
  • Account holders can freely withdraw or transfer funds internationally for approved purposes.
  • Banks must report large transactions to the CBN and the Nigerian Financial Intelligence Unit (NFIU).
  • The naira equivalent of dom account balances is subject to foreign exchange regulations.

It is important to understand the difference between a dom account and a naira account with a linked domiciliary card. The latter is a naira account that uses a Mastercard or Visa to make foreign currency transactions - the bank converts at the prevailing rate at the point of transaction. A true domiciliary account, by contrast, actually holds foreign currency. This distinction matters significantly when the naira is depreciating, because the foreign currency in your dom account retains its value relative to USD/GBP/EUR regardless of what happens to the naira.

Key Takeaway: A domiciliary account holds foreign currency (USD, GBP, EUR) inside Nigeria's banking system. It is regulated by the CBN and gives you a Nigerian SWIFT-enabled account for receiving international payments, holding forex savings, and making overseas transfers.

Who Needs a Domiciliary Account?

In 2026, the following five profiles of Nigerians benefit most from holding a domiciliary account:

1. Remote Workers and Freelancers Earning in USD

With Nigeria producing some of Africa's top software engineers, designers, and digital marketers, many Nigerians now earn in US dollars from platforms like Upwork, Toptal, Fiverr, and direct client contracts. A dom account provides a local Nigerian bank account that can receive SWIFT dollar transfers, which can then be accessed as cash or converted to naira at a favourable rate. Services like Grey (formerly Aboki), Lemfi (formerly Lemonade Finance), and Leatherback provide US/UK virtual account numbers that settle into your dom account.

2. Nigerian Diaspora Sending Remittances

Nigeria is one of Africa's top remittance recipients, with billions of dollars sent home annually. Diaspora Nigerians who want to send money to family without losing value to conversion can send directly to a dom account, allowing the recipient to hold USD and convert to naira at a time of their choosing - rather than being forced to convert immediately at a potentially unfavourable rate.

3. Importers Paying Overseas Suppliers

Business owners who import goods from China, the US, Europe, or the UAE regularly need to make international payments. A dom account enables outward wire transfers for Form M (import) transactions, Letters of Credit, and direct supplier payments. Without a dom account, importers must rely on their bank's bureau de change desk, which may have limited forex supply.

4. Students Paying School Fees Abroad

Nigerian students at UK, US, Canadian, and European universities need to make large tuition and accommodation payments in foreign currency. Parents can build up funds in a dom account over time, accumulating foreign currency gradually, and then wire the funds directly when fees are due - avoiding the need to scramble for forex at the official or parallel market at short notice.

5. Entrepreneurs and SMEs with International Revenue

E-commerce merchants selling on Etsy or Amazon, consultants invoicing international clients, and SaaS founders with foreign subscribers all benefit from a dom account as a central hub for their foreign currency revenue. It creates a clean separation between naira and foreign currency cash flows, simplifying accounting and tax reporting.

Step-by-Step Process to Open a Domiciliary Account

Follow these steps to open your domiciliary account successfully:

  1. Choose your bank: Start with the bank you already trust, but do not stop there. Compare branch accessibility, inward and outward transfer charges, whether references are still required, and how easy the account is to manage in the app.
  2. Gather your documents: Prepare your BVN, valid government-issued ID, proof of address, passport photo(s), and any other KYC item your bank requests. If a branch still expects references, get clarity before you leave home.
  3. Visit the branch in person: Many banks still handle final domiciliary onboarding in branch even when some forms can be started online. Bring originals and copies of your documents and go early enough to fix missing items the same day.
  4. Request the correct account type: Tell the customer service officer you want a foreign-currency domiciliary account and specify the currency you need most often, usually USD, GBP, or EUR.
  5. Complete and submit the form: Fill in the details carefully and make sure your name, phone number, address, and ID information match across your documents. Small inconsistencies create avoidable delays.
  6. Meet the opening balance requirement: Confirm the current minimum for your exact account type before funding it. Some banks expect the opening amount in foreign currency, while others may walk you through the next step after approval.
  7. Collect your account details: Once the account is approved, collect the account number, SWIFT details, and any transfer instructions you should share with overseas payers. Ask how long activation usually takes instead of assuming it is instant.
  8. Activate app access and alerts: Make sure the new account appears correctly on internet or mobile banking, and confirm whether credit alerts or transfer access need extra setup.

Tips to Maximize Your Domiciliary Account

Opening the account is only the first step. Here is how to make it genuinely useful instead of leaving it half-set up.

1. Always Maintain the Minimum Balance

Even when a bank does not advertise a dramatic penalty, running the account too close to zero makes it harder to stay compliant with whatever operating balance or branch process applies to that product. A small buffer saves stress.

2. Time Your Naira Conversions Strategically

One of the core benefits of a dom account is flexibility. Instead of converting every incoming payment immediately, you can watch the market and choose your timing more deliberately. The AfroTools Currency Converter helps you track live NGN rates while you decide.

3. Use Virtual USD Accounts for Receiving Payments

If you are paid frequently from abroad, compare your bank's direct SWIFT route with regulated payout platforms or international payment providers that your clients already use. The best setup depends on the payment rail, the fees, and how quickly you need the funds to land.

4. Keep Documentation for Compliance

Large deposits and transfers attract more questions than small routine flows. Keep invoices, contracts, school-fee letters, or supplier documents ready so you can answer source-of-funds questions quickly when the bank asks.

5. Use Multiple Currencies if You Have Multi-Currency Income

If you receive more than one currency regularly, ask whether the bank supports separate currency accounts cleanly. Holding each currency in its own lane is often simpler than converting too early and losing money on avoidable FX spreads.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Letting the account become inactive: Even if the bank does not frame it as a major issue, dormant or barely used accounts create unnecessary admin when you suddenly need them again.
  • Using incorrect SWIFT details: When sharing your account details for inbound wires, always provide the complete SWIFT code, account number, bank name, bank address, and your full legal name as registered on the account. A single mistake causes wire returns and delays of days to weeks.
  • Assuming instant credit: International transfers are rarely as fast as people hope. Build in extra time for bank checks, intermediary banks, and internal processing.
  • Ignoring tax obligations: Foreign currency income received into a dom account in Nigeria is still subject to Nigerian income tax (for individuals) or company income tax (for companies). Do not assume that keeping money in a dom account exempts it from tax.
  • Not comparing transfer charges: Small bank fees look harmless until they repeat across every incoming payment or outward transfer. Always ask for the current schedule of charges before you commit.

Frequently Asked Questions

What documents do I need to open a domiciliary account in Nigeria?

Start with the core checklist: BVN, valid government-issued ID, proof of address, passport photo(s), and a completed account-opening form. Some banks still ask for references or extra KYC support, so confirm the exact branch requirement before you visit.

What is the minimum balance for a domiciliary account in Nigeria?

Minimum opening balances vary by bank and account type. GTBank and Zenith publicly point to USD 100, GBP 100, or EUR 100 ranges, while FirstBank's standard domiciliary account page lists USD 100, EUR 100, GBP 50, and CFA 10,000. Always confirm the current figure with the bank before you go.

Can I withdraw cash from my domiciliary account?

Yes, branch cash withdrawals are part of the normal use case for domiciliary accounts. The practical issue is process, not permission: large withdrawals may need prior notice, extra identification, or source-of-funds checks.

Can I use my domiciliary account for international transfers?

Yes. A domiciliary account is built for inward and outward foreign-currency transfers. Inbound payments usually come through SWIFT, while outward transfers depend on the bank's documentation rules and the purpose of the payment.

What is the difference between a domiciliary account and a domiciliary savings account?

A standard domiciliary account is a current (checking) account with unlimited transactions - ideal for business and frequent use. A domiciliary savings account earns a small amount of interest but limits the number of monthly withdrawals (typically 6 or fewer). Most professionals and businesses use the current domiciliary account. The savings variant suits those primarily holding forex as a store of value with limited transaction needs.

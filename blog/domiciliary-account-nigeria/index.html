What is a Domiciliary Account?

A domiciliary account (commonly called a "dom account") is a foreign currency current account held at a Nigerian commercial bank. Unlike a standard naira savings or current account, a dom account holds balances denominated in a foreign currency — most commonly the US Dollar (USD), British Pound (GBP), or Euro (EUR). The funds in the account are owned by the account holder and are maintained in the foreign currency; they are not automatically converted to naira.

The Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN) regulates domiciliary accounts under the Foreign Exchange (Monitoring and Miscellaneous Provisions) Act and associated circulars. The CBN has, over the years, issued various directives governing how funds in dom accounts can be deposited, withdrawn, and transferred. Key CBN rules include:

  • Funds can be deposited via cash lodgement, inward transfers, or proceeds from export transactions.
  • Account holders can freely withdraw or transfer funds internationally for approved purposes.
  • Banks must report large transactions to the CBN and the Nigerian Financial Intelligence Unit (NFIU).
  • The naira equivalent of dom account balances is subject to foreign exchange regulations.

It is important to understand the difference between a dom account and a naira account with a linked domiciliary card. The latter is a naira account that uses a Mastercard or Visa to make foreign currency transactions — the bank converts at the prevailing rate at the point of transaction. A true domiciliary account, by contrast, actually holds foreign currency. This distinction matters significantly when the naira is depreciating, because the foreign currency in your dom account retains its value relative to USD/GBP/EUR regardless of what happens to the naira.

Key Takeaway: A domiciliary account holds foreign currency (USD, GBP, EUR) inside Nigeria's banking system. It is regulated by the CBN and gives you a Nigerian SWIFT-enabled account for receiving international payments, holding forex savings, and making overseas transfers.

Who Needs a Domiciliary Account?

In 2026, the following five profiles of Nigerians benefit most from holding a domiciliary account:

1. Remote Workers and Freelancers Earning in USD

With Nigeria producing some of Africa's top software engineers, designers, and digital marketers, tens of thousands of Nigerians now earn in US dollars from platforms like Upwork, Toptal, Fiverr, and direct client contracts. A dom account provides a local Nigerian bank account that can receive SWIFT dollar transfers, which can then be accessed as cash or converted to naira at a favourable rate. Services like Grey (formerly Aboki), Lemfi (formerly Lemonade Finance), and Leatherback provide US/UK virtual account numbers that settle into your dom account.

2. Nigerian Diaspora Sending Remittances

Nigeria is one of Africa's top remittance recipients, with billions of dollars sent home annually. Diaspora Nigerians who want to send money to family without losing value to conversion can send directly to a dom account, allowing the recipient to hold USD and convert to naira at a time of their choosing — rather than being forced to convert immediately at a potentially unfavourable rate.

3. Importers Paying Overseas Suppliers

Business owners who import goods from China, the US, Europe, or the UAE regularly need to make international payments. A dom account enables outward wire transfers for Form M (import) transactions, Letters of Credit, and direct supplier payments. Without a dom account, importers must rely on their bank's bureau de change desk, which may have limited forex supply.

4. Students Paying School Fees Abroad

Nigerian students at UK, US, Canadian, and European universities need to make large tuition and accommodation payments in foreign currency. Parents can build up funds in a dom account over time, accumulating foreign currency gradually, and then wire the funds directly when fees are due — avoiding the need to scramble for forex at the official or parallel market at short notice.

5. Entrepreneurs and SMEs with International Revenue

E-commerce merchants selling on Etsy or Amazon, consultants invoicing international clients, and SaaS founders with foreign subscribers all benefit from a dom account as a central hub for their foreign currency revenue. It creates a clean separation between naira and foreign currency cash flows, simplifying accounting and tax reporting.

Step-by-Step Process to Open a Domiciliary Account

Follow these steps to open your domiciliary account successfully:

  1. Choose your bank: Consider which bank you already have a naira account with, as this simplifies the process significantly. If you are a GTBank customer, opening a GTBank dom account is straightforward. If you are starting fresh, compare fees and proximity of branches.
  2. Gather your documents: Collect your valid government-issued ID (international passport is recommended as it is accepted by all banks), BVN, a recent utility bill (PHCN/EKEDC, LAWMA, LCC toll statement, or similar — dated within the last 3 months), and two recent passport photographs.
  3. Visit the branch in person: Domiciliary account opening almost always requires an in-person branch visit. Bring originals and photocopies of all documents. Visit early on a weekday morning to avoid long queues.
  4. Request the domiciliary account opening form: Inform the customer service officer that you want to open a foreign currency domiciliary account. Specify whether you want USD, GBP, or EUR (or multiple currencies).
  5. Complete and submit the form: Fill in all required fields carefully. Ensure your name matches exactly across all documents. Submit the form with your documents.
  6. Make the opening deposit: The minimum opening deposit is typically $100 (or equivalent). You can deposit foreign currency cash directly at the branch teller. Some banks allow an initial naira-funded opening if you do not yet have foreign currency cash.
  7. Collect your account details: Once processed (same day to 3 business days), you will receive your account number and SWIFT/routing details. These are what you share with overseas senders. Your account number is your Nigerian account number; the SWIFT code identifies your bank.
  8. Activate internet/mobile banking: Register your dom account on your bank's mobile app. This allows you to view balances, receive credit alerts, and initiate transfers without visiting a branch.

Tips to Maximize Your Domiciliary Account

Opening the account is only the first step. Here is how to get maximum value:

1. Always Maintain the Minimum Balance

Banks charge maintenance fees when your balance falls below the minimum. Keep at least $100–$200 as a buffer at all times. This is especially important if you have periods of irregular income (common for freelancers).

2. Time Your Naira Conversions Strategically

One of the core benefits of a dom account is that you can choose when to convert to naira. Monitor the CBN official rate and the parallel market rate. When there is a large spread in your favour — or when the naira appears to be strengthening temporarily — convert to naira. 3. Use Virtual USD Accounts for Receiving Payments

Services like Grey, Lemfi, Busha, and Leatherback provide US and UK virtual account numbers (ACH/sort code accounts) that receive payments from PayPal, Stripe, ACH transfers, and other platforms that do not support direct SWIFT. These services then route the funds to your dom account via a local bank transfer. This approach avoids the $10–$15 inward SWIFT fee on small frequent payments.

4. Keep Documentation for Compliance

For large deposits or transfers (generally above $10,000), banks are required by CBN and NFIU regulations to request source-of-funds documentation. Keep records of client contracts, invoices, and payment receipts so you can quickly satisfy AML/KYC requests and avoid account restrictions.

5. Use Multiple Currencies if You Have Multi-Currency Income

If you have income in both USD and GBP (common for UK-based Nigerian freelancers), open separate USD and GBP dom accounts. Converting GBP to USD before depositing incurs unnecessary FX costs. Hold each currency natively in its own account.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Letting the balance fall to zero: A zero-balance dom account is dormant after a period. Dormancy fees and account reactivation procedures are administrative headaches. Always maintain a small buffer.
  • Using incorrect SWIFT details: When sharing your account details for inbound wires, always provide the complete SWIFT code, account number, bank name, bank address, and your full legal name as registered on the account. A single mistake causes wire returns and delays of days to weeks.
  • Assuming instant credit: Inbound SWIFT transfers take 1–5 business days to credit. Plan payments from clients accordingly, especially for time-sensitive transactions.
  • Ignoring tax obligations: Foreign currency income received into a dom account in Nigeria is still subject to Nigerian income tax (for individuals) or company income tax (for companies). Do not assume that keeping money in a dom account exempts it from tax.
  • Not comparing inward transfer fees: $15 per inbound SWIFT at GTBank versus $10 at UBA may seem trivial, but for a freelancer receiving 20 payments per year, that is a $100 difference annually.

Frequently Asked Questions

What documents do I need to open a domiciliary account in Nigeria?

You typically need: a valid government-issued ID (national ID, international passport, or driver's licence), Bank Verification Number (BVN), a recent utility bill (not older than 3 months), two passport photographs, and a completed account opening form. Some banks (Zenith, First Bank) may also ask for a reference letter from an existing customer.

What is the minimum balance for a domiciliary account in Nigeria?

The standard minimum opening balance across all major Nigerian banks — GTBank, Zenith, Access Bank, UBA, and First Bank — is $100 (or the equivalent in GBP/EUR). Some banks waive this for premium or existing customers. Maintain at least this minimum to avoid monthly maintenance charges.

Can I withdraw cash from my domiciliary account?

Yes, you can withdraw foreign currency cash directly at your bank branch. CBN regulations permit reasonable cash withdrawals. However, very large cash withdrawals (generally above $10,000) may require prior notice and documentation. Some banks also issue USD debit cards that allow ATM withdrawals in foreign currency when travelling abroad.

Can I use my domiciliary account for international transfers?

Yes. A domiciliary account can both receive and send international SWIFT wire transfers. Inbound payments from overseas clients, diaspora family members, or international platforms route directly to your account using your SWIFT code and account number. Outbound transfers for approved purposes (imports, school fees, travel, investments) are also supported with the appropriate documentation.

What is the difference between a domiciliary account and a domiciliary savings account?

A standard domiciliary account is a current (checking) account with unlimited transactions — ideal for business and frequent use. A domiciliary savings account earns a small amount of interest but limits the number of monthly withdrawals (typically 6 or fewer). Most professionals and businesses use the current domiciliary account. The savings variant suits those primarily holding forex as a store of value with limited transaction needs.

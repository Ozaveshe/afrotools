Uganda payroll looks simple until a payslip starts mixing three different public charges that are not administered by the same institution. URA handles Pay As You Earn, NSSF handles mandatory retirement contributions, and local governments handle Local Service Tax. If you only remember the headline rates, it is easy to miss what should be withheld monthly, what is based on gross salary, and what depends on take-home salary after PAYE.

This guide keeps to official materials that were publicly live and checked on April 21, 2026. The main source set is URA's Taxation Handbook FY 2024/25, URA's 2025/26 sector guidance that reproduces the current PAYE formulas, NSSF Uganda's membership page, and KCCA's Local Service Tax FAQs. Where a rule is locally administered, this article says so instead of pretending Kampala guidance is a universal national payroll manual.

What Sits on a Uganda Payroll Line

For a resident employee, the normal monthly payroll conversation usually comes down to three items.

Item Who administers it How the official source describes it Why it matters
PAYE Uganda Revenue Authority URA's handbook says PAYE is a tax levied on gross salary on employees earning above UGX 235,000 per month It is the main monthly income tax withholding
NSSF National Social Security Fund NSSF says employers deduct 5% from the employee's gross monthly wage and add 10% as the employer portion It affects take-home pay and employer payroll cost
Local Service Tax Local government authority KCCA's FAQ says employee LST is based on take-home salary after PAYE and is assessed in annual bands It is often missed because it is not a simple monthly percentage

The first useful distinction is that these charges do not all start from the same base. URA's current handbook frames PAYE as tax on gross salary. NSSF also uses gross monthly wage. KCCA's LST FAQ, by contrast, says employee LST uses take-home salary after PAYE. If payroll software or a spreadsheet uses one base for all three, the result can be wrong even if the rates themselves look familiar.

Current Uganda PAYE Bands and Formulas

URA's currently published resident PAYE schedule still begins at a zero percent band up to UGX 235,000 per month. The thresholds below are the ones reproduced in URA's live sector guidance and reflected in the current URA handbook materials available on April 21, 2026.

Monthly chargeable income Resident PAYE formula What it means in practice
0 to UGX 235,000 0 No PAYE is due in this band
UGX 235,001 to UGX 335,000 10% of the amount above UGX 235,000 The first UGX 100,000 above the threshold is taxed at 10%
UGX 335,001 to UGX 410,000 UGX 10,000 + 20% of the amount above UGX 335,000 The second slice is taxed at 20%
UGX 410,001 to UGX 10,000,000 UGX 25,000 + 30% of the amount above UGX 410,000 Most professional salaries land here
Above UGX 10,000,000 [(CY - 410,000) x 30% + 25,000] + [(CY - 10,000,000) x 10%] The top slice effectively carries a 40% marginal burden

That last line is the one that most quick explainers gloss over. URA's published formula does not rewrite the whole tax schedule from scratch at UGX 10 million. It keeps the normal 30% structure that already applies above UGX 410,000 and then adds another 10% on the slice above UGX 10,000,000. That is why payroll teams often describe the top marginal rate as 40%.

Two practical consequences follow from URA's published structure.

  • The zero percent band is real. The first UGX 235,000 is not taxed for resident employees.
  • Crossing UGX 10,000,000 does not make the whole salary taxable at 40%. The higher rate only applies to the slice above that line.

This matters for salary negotiations and payroll checks. A worker moving from UGX 9.8 million to UGX 10.2 million monthly will see a higher marginal bite on only UGX 200,000 of extra income, not on the entire salary.

NSSF Rates and Remittance Timing

NSSF Uganda's current membership guidance is unusually clear. It says the employer must deduct 5% from the employee's total gross monthly wage and add 10% of the same gross monthly wage as the employer share, making a total contribution of 15%. The same page also says contributions must be paid by the 15th day of the following month.

NSSF checkpoint: The employee sees a 5% deduction, but the employer's total statutory NSSF payroll cost is 15% when the 10% employer portion is included.

For workers, the biggest practical question is usually take-home pay. NSSF reduces cash received because the employee portion is withheld from gross salary. For employers, the bigger operational point is timing. Missing the 15th means the payroll liability is no longer only an internal HR issue. It becomes a statutory remittance problem.

NSSF's mandatory coverage page also says the Fund covers employers regardless of employee count for eligible workers in the covered age band, except those under the government pensions scheme. That is relevant for small employers who still think NSSF obligations start only once a company grows. The current NSSF wording does not frame it that way.

Local Service Tax Bands and How They Are Applied

Local Service Tax is where many Uganda payroll explanations become too generic. KCCA's FAQ says an employee becomes eligible where monthly take-home salary exceeds UGX 100,000. The same FAQ defines take-home salary as gross salary after deducting PAYE. That detail matters because LST is not calculated on gross salary and not calculated after NSSF either.

KCCA's published employee band table is annual, not monthly. The charge is then handled through the employer salary schedule for assessment.

Above UGX 800,000 to UGX 900,000UGX 80,000

KCCA also says employees earning a salary or wage are handled in the first four months of every financial year. In practice, many payroll teams spread the annual amount across those months. The important point is not the spreading method by itself. The important point is that LST is an annual banded charge, not a flat monthly percentage like NSSF.

That is also why two employees with similar gross salaries can end up in different LST bands if PAYE changes their take-home pay differently. Once the annual band is set, the local authority assessment drives the amount due.

Employer Deadlines That Matter in Practice

Uganda payroll compliance problems often come from timing, not arithmetic. The key published deadlines in the official source set checked for this article are:

  • PAYE: URA's handbook says the employer withholds monthly and remits by the 15th day of the following month.
  • NSSF: NSSF's membership page says contributions are paid by the 15th day of the following month.
  • LST for employees in Kampala: KCCA says payment falls within the first four months of the financial year, with the employer submitting the salary schedule and LST computation for assessment.

That means a Uganda payroll calendar cannot be built around only one deadline. PAYE and NSSF align monthly around the 15th, while LST follows a local authority cycle. If an employer outsources payroll but keeps tax and pension remittance approvals in-house, this split is exactly where late payments can happen.

Mistakes That Create Payroll Surprises

These are the problems that show up most often when a Ugandan payslip is checked line by line against the official materials.

  • Treating LST like a monthly percentage. KCCA's published employee schedule is annual and banded. It is not another five percent style deduction.
  • Using the wrong LST base. KCCA says employee LST uses take-home salary after PAYE. A sheet that uses gross salary will misclassify some staff.
  • Forgetting the extra 10% above UGX 10 million. Salaries in the top band need the additional top-slice calculation.
  • Missing the matching 15th day deadlines. URA and NSSF both point to the 15th of the following month for remittance.
  • Assuming one Kampala rule explains every local government process. LST is locally administered. KCCA is the right source for Kampala employees, but employers in other jurisdictions should confirm local authority practice as well.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does Uganda PAYE start at UGX 235,000 or above it?

URA's published resident schedule keeps the first UGX 235,000 in the zero percent band. The next slice above that point is where PAYE starts.

Is NSSF deducted before PAYE in Uganda?

NSSF and PAYE are both calculated from gross salary in the current official guidance set used for this article. In practice, payroll teams usually compute PAYE from the published URA bands and deduct the employee's 5% NSSF share separately.

Can someone have zero PAYE but still pay Local Service Tax?

Yes. KCCA's FAQ says employee LST starts when take-home salary after PAYE exceeds UGX 100,000. That means a worker can remain in the PAYE zero band and still fall into the first LST band.

What is the highest annual employee Local Service Tax amount in KCCA's published table?

KCCA's employee band table tops out at UGX 100,000 per year for take-home salary above UGX 1,000,000.

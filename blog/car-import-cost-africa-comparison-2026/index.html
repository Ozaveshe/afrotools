Car Import Cost in Africa 2026: Kenya, Ghana, Nigeria, Tanzania, Uganda and Zambia Compared

Verification note: this guide was checked on 24 April 2026 against public guidance from Kenya Revenue Authority, Ghana Revenue Authority, Nigeria Customs Service, Tanzania Revenue Authority, Tanzania Bureau of Standards, Uganda Revenue Authority and Zambia Revenue Authority. It is a planning guide, not a customs ruling.

The easiest mistake in African car import budgeting is asking, "what is the duty?" as if duty is the whole bill. It is not. The real question is: what will this exact vehicle cost after valuation, taxes, inspection, port release, clearing, storage, inland delivery and registration?

The same imported car can produce different answers in each market because each country has a different valuation anchor, document workflow, inspection regime, levy stack and port geography.

There is no honest one-line "cheapest country" answer

A country can look cheap for one vehicle and expensive for another. A small petrol car, a hybrid SUV, a commercial pickup and a ten-year-old luxury sedan do not travel through the same risk profile. The tax base can change with engine size, year, model, value, vehicle use, route and inspection outcome.

A better comparison is to use the same vehicle in each calculator and watch which line moves the total. If Kenya jumps because the KRA valuation reference is higher than the invoice, that is a valuation issue. If Ghana looks high after statutory levies and port charges, that is a charge-stack issue. If Nigeria looks uncertain because documents and tariff treatment are not settled, that is a clearance-risk issue. If Zambia changes sharply after choosing a specific duty row, that is a schedule issue.

Kenya: valuation and compliance come before the bargain

Kenya is one of the clearest examples of why an invoice price is not enough. KRA's public motor vehicle guidance explains that importers need to check customs value, import duty, excise duty, VAT, rail development levy and import declaration fee. KRA also announced a revised Current Retail Selling Price schedule for used motor vehicle valuation effective from 1 July 2025.

The planning lesson is simple: a buyer should not assume that a low auction invoice will be accepted as the full valuation base. The Kenya car import cost guide explains the CRSP and Mombasa workflow in more detail, but the short version is that Kenya estimates need eligibility and valuation checks before price comparison.

Start with the vehicle age, first registration date, steering configuration, model details and KEBS inspection path. Then run the estimate. If you reverse the order, you can fall in love with a price before knowing whether the car can be cleared cleanly.

Ghana: ICUMS and port-side costs should be separated

Ghana Revenue Authority guidance points importers to ICUMS for checking vehicle duty using details such as chassis or VIN, model, year and make. GRA also explains that vehicle duty calculation uses a first purchase price, age-based depreciation, current exchange rates, freight and insurance.

For Ghana, the important comparison habit is to split official charges from practical port charges. Duty, VAT and statutory levy lines are not the same thing as terminal handling, safe bond rent, trade number plates, clearing agent fees and local delivery. A Ghana estimate that only models the official tax side can still be short of the cash needed for release.

Use the Ghana car import cost guide when you want the local checklist, then use the calculator to keep Tema, Takoradi and local dealer comparisons on the same footing.

Nigeria: document risk can become cost risk

Nigeria Customs Service guidance describes PAAR, the Pre-Arrival Assessment Report, as a mandatory import document used to assess goods and value before arrival. NCS import procedure material also lists documentation such as bill of lading, commercial invoice, Form M entry declaration, packing list and single goods declaration as part of the import process.

That makes Nigeria different from a simple duty-rate problem. The cost can move because the value changes, because the tariff classification changes, because the policy treatment changes, or because a document issue creates delay. Apapa and Tin Can port costs can make storage and release timing part of the landed cost.

The Nigeria car import cost guide is useful before you speak to a seller because it forces the document and PAAR questions into the budget. Do not wait until the vehicle is already on the water to discover that the numbers were incomplete.

Tanzania: TRA valuation and TBS inspection both matter

Tanzania Revenue Authority's used motor vehicle valuation workflow asks for vehicle details such as make, model, body, year, country of origin, fuel type and engine capacity. Tanzania Bureau of Standards destination inspection guidance also explains that a roadworthiness certificate is needed before TRA can proceed with registration.

That means Tanzania should be modeled in stages. First, check the vehicle identity against the TRA valuation path. Second, check the inspection and roadworthiness path. Third, add Dar es Salaam port clearance, storage, registration and inland delivery. A single rough tax percentage will hide too much.

The Tanzania car import cost guide walks through the UMVVS and Dar clearance logic. Use it when comparing a Japan direct import with a local dealer offer.

Uganda: corridor cost and valuation uncertainty are the watchpoints

Uganda Revenue Authority customs valuation material explains that the used motor vehicle valuation database is a basis for valuing used vehicles. In practical terms, that means the seller invoice is not the only number that matters.

Uganda also has a corridor question. Many vehicles reach Uganda through Mombasa or Dar es Salaam, and that route affects freight, inland delivery, timing and storage risk. A car can look attractive at the purchase stage and lose its advantage after corridor and registration costs are added.

The Uganda car import tax guide focuses on URA valuation, environmental levy checks, FX buffers and corridor planning. The calculator is best used with two routes and two exchange-rate assumptions.

Zambia: schedule matching is the hard part

Zambia Revenue Authority guidance explains value for duty purposes as the Kwacha equivalent of CIF value, including the price paid, insurance, freight and costs incidental to importation up to the port of entry in Zambia. ZRA also publishes used motor vehicle and motorcycle specific duty rates for 2026.

This makes Zambia a schedule-sensitive market. The wrong schedule row can make a neat estimate wrong. Engine size, vehicle type, category and current exchange rate matter, and long corridors from Dar, Durban or Walvis Bay can change the final delivered cost.

The Zambia car import duty guide explains why the calculator separates value for duty purposes, specific duty, other taxes and corridor delivery.

A practical formula for every country

Use this structure before making any payment:

  1. Vehicle purchase value: auction price, dealer price or negotiated seller price.
  2. Freight and insurance: shipping, marine insurance and route-specific movement.
  3. Customs value: the official or assessed value used by the revenue authority.
  4. Tax stack: duty, VAT, excise, specific duty, levies and import fees that apply in that country.
  5. Inspection and compliance: roadworthiness, standards inspection, age, steering, environmental treatment and document checks.
  6. Port and clearing costs: terminal handling, shipping-line charges, clearing agent work, storage, demurrage and release documents.
  7. Road-ready costs: inland delivery, registration, plates, insurance and local compliance.
  8. Buffer: exchange-rate movement, delay and correction costs.

The strongest import decision is not the one with the lowest first estimate. It is the one that still works after a conservative valuation, weaker exchange rate and realistic port-delay buffer.

When a local dealer may be safer than direct import

Direct import is not automatically better. It works best when the buyer understands the vehicle, has clean documents, can wait for shipping, can absorb exchange-rate movement and can verify the customs path before payment. A local dealer may be safer when the vehicle is near an age limit, the model has an unclear valuation reference, the buyer needs financing, or the final city is far from the port corridor.

Use the calculators to compare a direct import with a local offer on the same basis. Add the purchase price, expected tax stack, inspection, port charges, clearing, inland delivery, registration and a delay buffer. Then compare that result with the dealer's road-ready price, warranty position, inspection status and financing cost. If the direct import only wins by a small margin before delays and FX movement, the local car may be the stronger financial choice.

The best use of a car import calculator is not to prove that importing is always cheaper. It is to stop weak imports early. If the official valuation, inspection path or corridor cost makes the deal fragile, walk away before the vehicle leaves the origin country.

Bottom line

Do not compare African car import markets by headline duty alone. Compare the full landed-cost workflow. Kenya is highly valuation and compliance sensitive. Ghana needs a clean split between statutory and port-side costs. Nigeria needs document and PAAR discipline. Tanzania needs valuation plus inspection checks. Uganda needs corridor and FX buffers. Zambia needs careful schedule matching.

The right workflow is simple: pick the destination first, run the country calculator, verify the official source, ask a licensed clearing agent to confirm the current position, then decide whether the import still beats a local purchase.

West Africa: Nigeria, Ghana, Senegal, and Neighbours

West Africa presents some of the sharpest fuel price contrasts on the continent. Nigeria, Africa's largest oil producer, has seen dramatic price shifts since the removal of the petrol subsidy in 2023. Before deregulation, Nigerians paid around 185 naira per litre. By early 2026, pump prices hover between 850 and 1,100 naira per litre depending on location and supply conditions. The landing cost of imported refined petrol, the naira exchange rate against the dollar, and distribution logistics all contribute to these figures.

The Dangote Refinery, which began operations in Lagos, has started supplying the domestic market with refined petrol and diesel. This has reduced Nigeria's dependence on imported fuel and introduced some price competition. However, the refinery's pricing still tracks international benchmarks, meaning Nigerians are paying closer to true market rates than they did under the old subsidy regime.

Ghana regulates fuel prices through the National Petroleum Authority, with adjustments every two weeks. In 2026, petrol prices in Ghana sit around 15 to 17 cedis per litre. The country imports nearly all its refined fuel, and the cedi's performance against the dollar plays a major role in pump prices. Senegal and Cote d'Ivoire maintain regulated pricing with moderate government subsidies, keeping prices somewhat stable but still among the higher in the region. East Africa: Kenya, Tanzania, Uganda, Ethiopia

Kenya uses a monthly pricing formula managed by the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA). Each month, EPRA calculates the maximum retail price based on the average cost of imported fuel over the previous month, plus taxes, levies, and distribution margins. In early 2026, super petrol in Nairobi costs approximately 210 to 225 Kenya shillings per litre. Diesel is slightly cheaper at 195 to 210 shillings per litre.

Kenya's fuel prices include several levies that push costs higher: the Road Maintenance Levy, the Petroleum Development Levy, excise duty, and VAT at 16%. These combined taxes and levies can account for over 40% of the final pump price. Tanzania has a similar pricing structure but generally achieves slightly lower prices due to different tax rates and its proximity to the Dar es Salaam port.

Uganda, being landlocked, faces additional transport costs. Fuel arrives via pipeline from Kenya or by road tanker, adding a premium of 10 to 20% over Kenyan prices. Ethiopia has historically subsidised fuel heavily, but recent economic reforms and foreign currency shortages have pushed prices upward. Ethiopian petrol prices in 2026 sit around 70 to 80 birr per litre, though availability remains inconsistent in some regions.

Southern Africa: South Africa, Zimbabwe, Mozambique

South Africa has Africa's most transparent fuel pricing system. The Department of Mineral Resources and Energy adjusts prices on the first Wednesday of each month using the Basic Fuel Price (BFP), which tracks international refined petroleum prices and the rand-dollar exchange rate. In March 2026, petrol (95 unleaded, inland) costs approximately R24 to R26 per litre, while diesel ranges from R22 to R24 per litre.

South African fuel prices include significant levies: the general fuel levy, the Road Accident Fund levy, and various margins for wholesalers and retailers. These levies fund road infrastructure and accident compensation but add substantially to the final price. Zimbabwe presents a unique situation with fuel prices quoted in both USD and Zimbabwe dollars. Most fuel stations prefer USD payments, with petrol costing around $1.40 to $1.60 per litre. Mozambique benefits from its coastal position and the Maputo refinery, keeping prices moderate by regional standards.

North Africa and Oil-Producing Nations

North African oil producers enjoy some of the cheapest fuel on the continent. Libya, despite ongoing political instability, maintains heavily subsidised fuel prices that are among the lowest in the world, often under $0.10 per litre. Algeria similarly subsidises domestic fuel, with petrol costing around 45 to 50 Algerian dinars per litre, equivalent to roughly $0.30 USD.

Egypt has been gradually reducing fuel subsidies as part of IMF-backed economic reforms. Petrol prices have risen significantly over the past three years, with 92-octane petrol now costing around 13.50 to 15 Egyptian pounds per litre. Morocco and Tunisia, which import most of their fuel, have prices closer to European levels, making them among the most expensive in Africa.

Angola, a major oil producer in sub-Saharan Africa, maintains subsidised fuel prices but has been reducing subsidies progressively. Petrol in Luanda costs around 300 to 350 kwanzas per litre, still well below what it would cost at full market price.

What Drives Fuel Price Differences Across Africa?

Several key factors explain why a litre of petrol can cost ten times more in one African country than another:

Government subsidies are the single biggest factor. Oil-producing nations like Libya, Algeria, and Angola use petroleum revenue to keep domestic prices artificially low. Countries that have removed subsidies, like Nigeria and Egypt, have seen dramatic price increases.

Refining capacity matters enormously. Most African countries import refined petroleum products because they lack domestic refining infrastructure. The cost of importing refined fuel includes international shipping, insurance, port charges, and distribution, all of which add to the final price. Countries with operational refineries, even partial ones, can reduce this import premium.

Taxes and levies vary dramatically. In Kenya and South Africa, government levies can account for 35 to 45% of the pump price. In subsidised markets, taxes on fuel are minimal or nonexistent. These policy choices reflect different government priorities around revenue collection, infrastructure funding, and social welfare.

Currency exchange rates play a critical role because crude oil and refined products are traded in US dollars. When the naira, cedi, or shilling weakens against the dollar, the local currency cost of imported fuel rises even if the dollar price of oil stays flat. This is why fuel prices in Nigeria and Ghana have risen sharply during periods of currency depreciation.

Landlocked geography adds transport costs. Countries like Uganda, Rwanda, and Zambia pay more because fuel must travel longer distances overland from coastal ports.

How to Budget for Fuel Costs in 2026

With fuel prices remaining volatile across the continent, effective budgeting is more important than ever. Here are practical strategies for managing fuel expenses:

Track your actual consumption by recording litres purchased and kilometres driven over a full month. For businesses that rely on transport, fuel costs should be reviewed weekly against revenue. Logistics companies operating across borders need to factor in price differences between countries when planning routes. A truck route through a country with cheaper fuel can save significant money over time, even if the distance is slightly longer.

Generator users in countries with unreliable power supply should calculate fuel costs as a fixed monthly expense. In Nigeria, where many homes and businesses run generators for several hours daily, generator fuel can be one of the largest household expenses. Outlook: Where Are African Fuel Prices Heading?

Several trends will shape fuel prices across Africa for the rest of 2026. Global crude oil prices remain the dominant factor, with OPEC+ production decisions and geopolitical tensions influencing benchmark prices. If Brent crude stays in the $75 to $85 per barrel range, most African countries can expect relative price stability.

The expansion of domestic refining capacity, particularly Nigeria's Dangote Refinery reaching full output, could reduce import dependency and create downward pressure on prices in West Africa. Kenya and Tanzania are exploring new refinery projects, though these are years away from production.

Currency depreciation remains the biggest risk for fuel price increases. Countries with weak or volatile currencies will continue to see fuel prices rise even during periods of stable global oil prices. Governments may face pressure to reintroduce subsidies during election periods, but the fiscal cost of fuel subsidies makes long-term subsidisation unsustainable for most African economies.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which African country has the cheapest fuel?

Libya, Algeria, and Angola typically have the lowest fuel prices in Africa thanks to domestic oil production and government subsidies. Libya often sells petrol for under $0.10 per litre, making it among the cheapest globally.

Why are fuel prices so different across African countries?

The main drivers are government subsidies, taxes and levies, refining capacity, transportation costs for landlocked nations, currency exchange rates, and whether a country produces crude oil domestically or imports refined products.

How often do fuel prices change in Africa?

It varies by country. Nigeria adjusts prices periodically through the NNPCL, Kenya reviews monthly via EPRA, South Africa adjusts on the first Wednesday of each month, and Ghana reviews every two weeks through the NPA. Some countries allow daily market-driven changes.

Does Nigeria still subsidise fuel?

Nigeria officially removed the petrol subsidy in mid-2023. Since then, prices have risen substantially and now fluctuate based on crude oil prices, the naira exchange rate, and distribution costs. The government has occasionally intervened to stabilise prices during sharp spikes.

Kenya Car Import Cost Guide 2026

Verification note: this guide was refreshed on 24 April 2026 against Kenya Revenue Authority import guidance, KRA's 2025 CRSP notice and the live AfroTools calculator structure. It is written for budgeting before you buy, not as a replacement for a KRA customs assessment.

The Kenya Car Import Cost Calculator is built for the common Japan to Mombasa workflow, but it also helps compare UAE, UK, South Africa and local dealer routes. The important thing is to model the whole landed cost, not only the auction price. A vehicle that looks cheap before shipping can become expensive after KRA valuation, duties, levies, inspection, port storage, clearing fees and inland delivery are added.

Start with eligibility before price

Kenya has rules that can block an import even when the budget looks workable. KRA guidance points importers to three practical checks before clearance: the vehicle should be within the used-vehicle age rule, it should be right-hand drive unless it falls into a narrow special-purpose exception, and it should go through the required roadworthiness inspection path. If a car fails one of those checks, a low purchase price does not save the deal.

The safest workflow is to confirm the year of manufacture, first registration date, steering configuration and export inspection path before you send a deposit. The calculator should be used after that eligibility screen, not before it.

CRSP matters more than many buyers expect

KRA announced a revised Current Retail Selling Price schedule for used motor vehicle valuation effective 1 July 2025. In practice, that means the customs value of a used import may be anchored to the KRA reference framework rather than to the price written on the seller invoice. If you are comparing two cars with similar auction prices, the one with the higher CRSP reference can still produce a higher tax bill.

When using the calculator, do not only enter the price you negotiated. Also keep a note of the model, trim, engine size, year and first registration details you will use with your clearing agent. Those are the details that help reconcile your estimate with the KRA valuation workflow.

What buyers usually miss

  • KRA valuation can be different from the invoice value used by the exporter.
  • Excise, VAT, import declaration fees and rail development levies can stack on top of duty.
  • KEBS inspection and roadworthiness documents are part of the clearance path, not optional extras.
  • Demurrage and storage risk grows when documents arrive late or the clearing process stalls.
  • Inland delivery beyond Mombasa should be part of the landed cost before you compare with local dealers.

Formula walkthrough

A useful planning formula is: estimated landed cost equals vehicle purchase cost plus freight and insurance, plus the customs assessment, plus inspection and port charges, plus clearing and inland delivery. Keep each line separate. If you combine everything into one "duty" number, it becomes harder to see whether the risk is coming from valuation, taxes, port delay or delivery.

For a pre-purchase screen, run a conservative estimate, then run a second version with a weaker shilling or higher customs value. If the vehicle only works under the most optimistic case, it is not a strong import candidate.

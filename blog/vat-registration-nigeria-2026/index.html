Nigeria's VAT Rate and How It Works

Nigeria charges 7.5% VAT on taxable goods and services. That rate hasn't changed since February 2020, when the Finance Act bumped it up from 5%. If you're running a business in Nigeria and you're selling anything that isn't explicitly exempt, you're collecting VAT on behalf of the Federal Inland Revenue Service (FIRS). Simple as that.

Here's what trips people up. VAT isn't your money. You collect it from customers, deduct whatever input VAT you paid on business purchases, and remit the difference to FIRS. If you collected NGN 500,000 in output VAT and paid NGN 200,000 in input VAT on supplies, you owe FIRS NGN 300,000. Not complicated in theory. The paperwork is where it gets annoying.

Every VAT-registered business must issue VAT invoices showing the 7.5% charge as a separate line item. No hiding it inside the price. Your invoice needs your TIN, business name, the customer's details, and the VAT amount clearly broken out.

The NGN 25M Threshold

Not every business needs to register. The threshold sits at NGN 25 million in annual turnover. If your total sales in 12 months don't cross that line, FIRS doesn't require you to register for VAT.

But here's the catch. "Turnover" means total revenue, not profit. A business doing NGN 30 million in sales but only making NGN 2 million in profit still needs to register. Revenue counts, not what's left after expenses.

Can you register voluntarily if you're under the threshold? Yes. Some businesses do this to reclaim input VAT on expensive equipment or supplies. If you're buying a lot of taxable inputs, voluntary registration might actually save you money. Talk to your accountant before deciding.

One more thing. If you cross the NGN 25M threshold at any point during the year, you've got six months to register with FIRS. Don't wait until year-end and hope nobody notices.

FIRS Registration Steps

The registration process is fully online through the FIRS TaxPro Max portal. No more queuing at tax offices unless something goes wrong with your application. Here's the step-by-step breakdown.

StepActionDetails
1Get a TINApply for a Tax Identification Number at the nearest FIRS office or via the Joint Tax Board portal if you don't have one
2Create TaxPro Max accountVisit taxpromax.firs.gov.ng and sign up using your TIN, email, and phone number
3Complete VAT registration formFill in business details: name, address, CAC registration number, nature of business, estimated annual turnover
4Upload documentsCAC certificate, utility bill, board resolution (for companies), passport photo of directors
5Submit and waitFIRS reviews within 5–10 working days. You'll get your VAT registration number via email
6Start charging VATIssue VAT invoices from your registration effective date. File monthly returns by the 21st

The whole process takes about two weeks if your documents are in order. Missing documents are the number one reason applications stall. Double-check everything before you hit submit.

Exempt and Zero-Rated Items

Not everything attracts VAT. Nigeria maintains two categories of relief: exempt items (no VAT charged, no input VAT recovery) and zero-rated items (0% VAT charged, but you can recover input VAT). The distinction matters for your cash flow.

Basic FoodUnprocessed agricultural products, rice, beans, yams, vegetables, fruitsExempt

Look, the exempt list catches a lot of new business owners off guard. If you're running a pharmacy, you don't charge VAT on drugs. But if that same pharmacy sells cosmetics or perfumes, those are taxable at 7.5%. Same shop, different rules depending on the product. Keep your records tight.

Monthly Filing and Deadlines

VAT returns are filed monthly. Not quarterly. Not annually. Monthly.

The deadline is the 21st day of the month following the transaction month. So if you made sales in January, your VAT return and payment are due by February 21st. Miss that date and penalties start accumulating immediately.

Filing happens on TaxPro Max. You'll need to prepare a summary of your output VAT (what you charged customers) and input VAT (what you paid on business purchases). The system calculates the difference. You pay the net amount through any approved bank or the Remita platform.

Even if you had zero transactions in a given month, you still need to file a nil return. FIRS doesn't accept silence as a substitute for paperwork. No sales? File anyway.

Okay so here's a practical tip. Set a calendar reminder for the 15th of every month. That gives you six days of buffer before the 21st deadline. Don't be the person scrambling on the 20th trying to reconcile three months of invoices.

Penalties for Non-Compliance

FIRS doesn't play around with VAT compliance. The penalties are specific, published, and they add up fast.

Failure to register: NGN 50,000 for the first month, then NGN 25,000 for every additional month you remain unregistered. If you ignore this for a year, that's NGN 325,000 in penalties before they even look at the actual VAT you owe.

Late filing: NGN 50,000 for the first month, NGN 25,000 per subsequent month. This is separate from late payment penalties.

Late payment: 5% per annum interest on the outstanding VAT amount, calculated from the due date until payment.

Failure to issue VAT invoices: NGN 50,000 penalty. Every invoice.

The thing is, these penalties are cumulative. Late to register AND late to file AND late to pay? You're looking at all three penalty streams running simultaneously. A small business that ignores VAT for 12 months could easily rack up over NGN 1 million in penalties alone, not counting the actual tax owed.

Don't test FIRS on this. The TaxPro Max system tracks everything automatically now, and they've gotten much better at enforcement since going digital.

Frequently Asked Questions

Nigeria's VAT rate is 7.5%. It's been at this level since February 2020 when it was increased from 5% under the Finance Act 2019. The rate applies to all taxable goods and services supplied within Nigeria.

The threshold is NGN 25 million in annual turnover. Businesses earning less than this aren't required to register, though they can do so voluntarily to reclaim input VAT on purchases.

Key exempt categories include basic food items (unprocessed agricultural products), medical and pharmaceutical supplies, educational materials, baby products, agricultural equipment, and financial services like loan interest and insurance premiums.

VAT returns must be filed and remitted by the 21st day of the month following the transaction month. January's VAT is due by February 21st, and so on. Nil returns must also be filed even if no transactions occurred.

FIRS charges NGN 50,000 for the first month of non-registration and NGN 25,000 for each additional month. These penalties accumulate alongside separate fines for late filing (NGN 50,000 first month, NGN 25,000 per subsequent month) and 5% annual interest on unpaid VAT.

Tanzania VAT is one of those rule sets where the headline rate is easy, but the current operational detail is not. The base law still says 18% is the standard rate. Finance Act 2025 then added a 16% route for some standard-rated supplies made to unregistered buyers who pay through a bank or an electronic payment system approved by the Commissioner General. The complication is that TRA's own public notice dated September 2, 2025 said the reduced-rate arrangement still depended on a further notice specifying the eligible persons and the implementation method.

That means a lot of Tanzania VAT content published after July 2025 became sloppy very quickly. Some pages now talk as if 16% replaced 18% across the board. Others still ignore the Finance Act change entirely. The safer reading, based on the primary source set available on April 19, 2026, is that 18% remains the general default rate and the 16% route should be treated as conditional, not universal, unless you have a clear basis showing the transaction fits the Commissioner General's implementation framework.

This guide sticks to what the law and TRA actually say. It covers the rate structure, the registration threshold, the filing date, the weekend rule, the receipt and EFD control environment, and the new withholding VAT mechanics. Current VAT Rates And The 16% Problem

The current Tanzania VAT Act still states that the amount of VAT payable is calculated using a rate of 18% for standard-rated supplies. Zero-rated supplies remain at 0%. That is the base position.

Finance Act 2025 changed the picture, but only in a narrow way. The law inserted a new subsection saying that where a standard-rated supply is made in Mainland Tanzania to an unregistered person in Mainland Tanzania who pays through a bank or electronic payment system approved by the Commissioner General, the rate shall be 16%. The same amendment says the subsection came into operation on September 1, 2025.

That sounds straightforward until you read TRA's public notice issued the next day. In the official notice dated September 2, 2025, TRA said the effective application of that 16% rule depended on a public notice to be issued by the Commissioner General specifying the persons eligible and the manner in which the arrangement would be implemented. In the same notice, TRA said the Commissioner General was still working on those requirements and that the public would be informed once finalized.

That combination matters. The law created the mechanism. TRA then said implementation still needed an eligibility and process notice. Based on the source set we could verify on April 19, 2026, the safest operational reading is this:

  • Treat 18% as the default rate for ordinary Tanzania VAT calculations.
  • Use 16% only where the transaction clearly fits the legal conditions and you have a defensible basis that the Commissioner General's implementation requirements are satisfied.
  • Do not market 16% as a blanket Tanzania VAT rate.

This is exactly where stale calculators create risk. A calculator that tells every user Tanzania VAT is now 16% is oversimplifying the law. A calculator that ignores the new section entirely is also incomplete. The right posture is conditionality.

A simple formula check shows why the distinction matters. On a TZS 1,000,000 taxable sale, 18% VAT is TZS 180,000, while 16% VAT is TZS 160,000. That TZS 20,000 difference is material. It should not be applied casually.

Registration Threshold And When It Bites

Tanzania's registration threshold is not stated directly in the VAT Act itself. Section 28 works by referring to the registration threshold, and the Value Added Tax (General) Regulations, 2015 set that threshold at TZS 100 million.

Section 28 of the VAT Act matters because it tells you how that threshold is tested. A person is required to register if the person's taxable turnover:

  • is equal to or greater than the registration threshold over the previous twelve months, or
  • is equal to or greater than half of the registration threshold over the previous six months.

Once you combine section 28 with Regulation 14, the practical triggers become:

  • TZS 100 million over the relevant twelve-month lookback, or
  • TZS 50 million over the relevant six-month lookback.

That six-month test is easy to miss, especially when someone quotes only the annual threshold. If a business crosses TZS 50 million in taxable turnover over six consecutive months, the law is already asking a registration question. It is not necessary to wait for the annual total to hit TZS 100 million before taking the issue seriously.

The regulations also preserve a route for intending traders. Regulation 13 says an intending trader can be registered where documentary evidence shows that the applicant will make taxable supplies and attain the registration threshold within twelve months from the date taxable supplies begin. In practice, that matters for businesses with a real launch plan, material startup spend, and a near-term move into taxable sales.

Practical rule: If your taxable turnover is around TZS 100 million annually or TZS 50 million over six months, stop treating VAT registration as a future issue. It is already a current compliance workflow.

Filing, Payment Dates, And Receipts

The current VAT Act is useful here because it resolves one of the common deadline mistakes. The law says the return is due on the 20th day after the end of the tax period. It then adds a practical relief rule: where the 20th day falls on a Saturday, Sunday, or public holiday, the return is lodged on the first working day after that.

That is the date you should build into your internal calendar. A March VAT period means a return due on April 20, unless April 20 falls on a non-working day, in which case the next working day applies.

Payment follows the same cycle. The VAT Act says that where the net amount for the period is positive, it is accounted for and paid at the time the VAT return is due to be filed. So the return date and payment discipline should be treated as one monthly control, not two separate afterthoughts.

On the operational side, TRA continues to run a live Taxpayer Portal, a public receipt verification portal, and EFD receipt APIs. The Tax Administration (General) Regulations also require users to issue fiscal receipts or fiscal invoices generated by their Electronic Fiscal Device for supplies made to customers. The practical implication is that invoice support is not optional admin polish. It is part of the audit trail that supports VAT reporting.

A safe monthly Tanzania VAT workflow therefore looks like this:

  1. Check whether the transaction is ordinary 18%, zero-rated, or only potentially eligible for the conditional 16% route.
  2. Reconcile output VAT and allowable input tax for the period.
  3. Confirm that fiscal receipts or invoices and payment records are in order.
  4. File by the 20th day, or the next working day if the 20th is not a working day.
  5. Pay the positive net amount by the same deadline.

Withholding VAT After Finance Act 2025

Finance Act 2025 added another change that Tanzania VAT explainers should not ignore: withholding VAT for supplies made to withholding agents. The amended section says that where a standard-rated supply is made to a withholding agent, the rate remains 18%, but the withholding agent must withhold 3% on supplies of goods and 6% on supplies of services. The law then says the rate payable to the taxable person supplying services shall be 12%.

The important operational point is that this is not a new general VAT rate. It is a cash-flow and remittance rule attached to the identity of the buyer. The transaction remains inside an 18% VAT framework, but the withholding agent remits part of that VAT differently.

Finance Act 2025 also added a documentation rule in section 70. A taxable person cannot subtract withheld output tax in the VAT calculation unless, at the time of filing the return for the relevant period, that person holds a valid value added tax withholding certificate. The same section says a person applying the 16% route under section 5(6) must submit proof of bank or electronic payment through the system or in the manner directed by the Commissioner General.

Those two compliance steps matter because they show the 2025 reforms are not just about changing percentages. They also tighten the documentation chain around reduced-rate treatment and withholding credits.

Stale Tanzania VAT Claims To Stop Repeating

The fastest way to improve a Tanzania VAT workflow in 2026 is to stop repeating claims that were already shaky by late 2025.

  • Stale claim one: Tanzania VAT is now simply 16%. The current primary source set does not support that blanket statement. The law created a conditional 16% route, but TRA's September 2, 2025 notice said implementation still required a further Commissioner General notice.
  • Stale claim two: Every Tanzania VAT return is due on the last working day of the next month. The current VAT Act text available on TanzLII says the return is due on the 20th day after the end of the tax period, with the next-working-day relief if the 20th falls on a weekend or public holiday.
  • Stale claim three: The annual threshold is the only registration trigger. Section 28 also uses half the threshold over six months, so the annual number alone is not the full test.
  • Stale claim four: Withholding VAT is just another reduced rate. It is not. It is a withholding mechanism attached to transactions with withholding agents.

If your calculator, memo, or ERP note still uses those assumptions, update it before the next return cycle. The arithmetic may still work, but the compliance logic behind it will be weaker than it should be.

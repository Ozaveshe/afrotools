Uganda VAT is straightforward at the headline level and much less straightforward once you start filing it. The rate is easy to remember. The hard part is knowing when a business should register, when a supply is zero-rated instead of exempt, how EFRIS changes your invoice workflow, and which deadline is current when old checklists are still floating around in finance folders.

This guide keeps to official Uganda Revenue Authority material checked on April 18, 2026. The core source set is URA's Value Added Tax FY 2023-24 A Simplified Guide, URA's VAT overview page, URA's September 13, 2024 EFRIS explainer, and the URA EFRIS handbook page. Where the sources overlap, this article uses the shared rule. Where one source is more precise, this article says so.

What Uganda VAT Covers

URA's current VAT guide says VAT is an indirect tax on consumption charged on value added at different stages in the chain of distribution. In Uganda, VAT is imposed on taxable supplies of goods and services made by a taxable person, and on imports other than exempt imports.

That sounds theoretical, but the practical consequence is simple. If a business is registered or required to register for VAT, it does not just need the correct rate. It needs the correct classification. The classification determines whether the supply is standard rated, zero-rated, or exempt, whether the seller should be in the VAT system at all, and whether the seller can claim input VAT on business purchases.

URA's simplified guide gives ordinary examples of taxable supplies, including sale of business assets, hiring or leasing goods for consideration, commission income, and even taking goods out of the business for own use. The underlying lesson is that VAT analysis follows the supply, not just the invoice label.

Current Uganda VAT Rates and Supply Types

The current standard VAT rate in Uganda remains 18%. URA's own materials separate Uganda supplies into three buckets that matter for compliance.

Supply type Rate What URA says Practical effect
Standard rated 18% URA's VAT guide says standard rated supplies are charged VAT at 18% Seller charges output VAT and can usually claim qualifying input VAT
Zero-rated 0% URA's guide lists examples including exports and some Uganda-manufactured drugs; URA's 2024 EFRIS explainer says zero-rated suppliers still file nil returns Supply stays in the VAT system even though output VAT is 0%
Exempt No VAT charged URA lists examples such as educational services, financial services, passenger transportation, social welfare services, and petroleum fuels Business dealing only in exempt supplies is generally not expected to register for VAT

The distinction between zero-rated and exempt is one of the most important Uganda VAT concepts. Both can look like "no VAT" to the customer, but they are not the same for the business. URA's guide is explicit: a person dealing only in exempt supplies is not expected to register for VAT, while a person dealing in zero-rated supplies is expected to register if they meet the registration requirements.

That one distinction affects registration, invoicing, returns, and whether input VAT can be carried through the business. It is the first thing worth checking if a supply seems to sit in a gray area.

Registration Threshold and When URA Expects Registration

URA's current rule is not just an annual number. The guide says a person carrying on business activities, or intending to do so, is required to apply for VAT registration if taxable turnover for three consecutive calendar months exceeds or is likely to exceed UGX 37.5 million. URA also presents the annual registration threshold as UGX 150 million.

That means registration is monitored on a rolling three-month basis, not only after a full year closes. URA says you should look at two periods continuously:

  • The past three consecutive calendar months
  • The next three consecutive calendar months, if the business reasonably expects the threshold to be exceeded

A formula walkthrough makes the rule easier to see:

Period Taxable turnover Result
Three consecutive months UGX 36,000,000 Below the compulsory threshold on URA's current rule
Three consecutive months UGX 39,000,000 Above UGX 37.5 million, so compulsory registration is triggered
Projected next three months Likely above UGX 37.5 million URA says expected turnover can also trigger registration

URA also allows voluntary registration. The guide says the Commissioner General may register a person voluntarily, but the applicant needs to satisfy URA on points such as a fixed place of business, proper record keeping, and the ability to submit regular and reliable returns. In other words, voluntary registration is possible, but it is not just a box-ticking exercise.

Registration checkpoint: If your taxable turnover is close to the three-month threshold, you should track it monthly instead of waiting for year-end. That is the cleanest way to avoid crossing into compulsory registration without noticing.

How to Calculate VAT Inclusive, Exclusive, and Reverse VAT

The standard Uganda VAT math is not difficult, but teams still make mistakes when the quoted amount already includes VAT. The formulas below use the current 18% rate.

Use case Formula Example result
Add VAT to a net price Net amount x 18% UGX 1,000,000 net gives UGX 180,000 VAT and UGX 1,180,000 gross
Extract VAT from a gross amount Gross x 18 / 118 UGX 1,180,000 gross contains UGX 180,000 VAT
Find the net from a VAT-inclusive amount Gross / 1.18 UGX 1,180,000 gross gives UGX 1,000,000 net

This is why reverse VAT calculators divide by 1.18 rather than simply multiplying by 18%. Once the number already includes VAT, the tax component is embedded in the total.

EFRIS, Invoices, and Monthly Filing Deadlines

URA's current simplified VAT guide says VAT-registered taxpayers are required to enroll for the Electronic Fiscal Receipting and Invoicing Solution, or EFRIS, and issue invoices and receipts online. URA's EFRIS handbook materials also say EFRIS is not itself a tax. URA describes it as a system that helps track VAT-related business records and invoices.

For businesses that are already VAT-registered, the operational consequence is important. VAT compliance is no longer only about calculating output tax at month end. It also affects invoice issuance, online receipt records, and whether the underlying transaction documentation lines up with URA's electronic record trail.

The current deadline language is also clearer than some older Uganda VAT checklists. Both URA's simplified VAT guide and URA's September 13, 2024 article on EFRIS say the business should:

  • File the VAT return within 15 days after the end of the month
  • Pay VAT due within 15 days after the end of the month
  • Maintain proper records
  • Issue tax invoices or e-invoices through the required process

That 15-day rule matters because older pages and secondary summaries can still quote a 20th-of-the-following-month deadline. For this article, the 15-day rule is the one supported directly by the current URA guide and URA's own 2024 explainer, both checked again on April 18, 2026.

Input VAT, Refunds, and What Zero-Rated Really Means

URA's guide defines input tax as VAT paid or payable on purchases or imports of taxable goods or services by a taxable person, and output tax as the VAT charged by the taxable person when making taxable supplies. In practice, Uganda VAT returns are built around the difference between those two numbers.

If output VAT is higher than input VAT, the difference is VAT payable to URA. If input VAT is higher than output VAT, URA says the difference is claimable. The guide says the taxpayer may choose to seek a refund from URA if the excess is more than UGX 5 million, offset it against VAT payable, or carry it forward against future VAT.

This is another reason the zero-rated versus exempt distinction matters. URA's materials treat zero-rated supplies as taxable supplies inside the VAT system. That means a zero-rated exporter can still have input VAT and a nil output position. Exempt supplies are different, because they sit outside that recoverability logic in a much more restrictive way.

Penalties and Compliance Mistakes That Get Expensive

URA's simplified guide includes a practical penalty table, and the message is consistent: late registration and late filing get expensive faster than many small businesses expect.

Issue What URA's guide says
Failure to register when required URA says the taxpayer is liable to penal tax equal to double the amount of tax payable during the period they remained unregistered
Late VAT return URA says the penal tax is the greater of UGX 200,000 or interest at 2% per month compounded for the period the return is outstanding
Late payment of VAT due URA says unpaid tax attracts penal tax at 2%, compounded

The costly mistakes usually come from process failures, not from hard tax theory. Common examples include:

  • Watching annual turnover but not the rolling three-month threshold
  • Treating exempt and zero-rated supplies as the same thing
  • Issuing documentation outside the EFRIS process after VAT registration
  • Using a stale monthly deadline copied from an old checklist
  • Assuming input VAT is claimable without checking that the underlying supply stays inside the VAT system

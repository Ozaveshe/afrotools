If you sell goods or services in Uganda, VAT stops being a background tax issue very quickly. Once your business grows past the registration tests, URA expects monthly returns, VAT-compliant invoices, and a workflow that can stand up to review. The practical problem is that many businesses know the headline rate but not the registration trigger, the EFRIS requirement, or the exact filing date.

This guide focuses on the current Uganda rules that are most likely to affect a growing business in 2026: the 18% standard rate, the UGX 150 million annual test, the UGX 37.5 million three-month test, the monthly return due on the 15th, and URA's position that VAT-registered taxpayers must register for EFRIS. Current Uganda VAT rate and what VAT covers

Uganda's standard VAT rate is 18%. In plain terms, this is the rate most taxable domestic goods and services fall under unless the law specifically places them in the zero-rated or exempt schedules. URA's guidance is clear on the basic architecture: VAT is charged on taxable supplies, registered businesses collect output tax from customers, and they offset eligible input tax from business purchases before paying the net position.

That sounds simple, but three practical distinctions matter:

  • Standard-rated supplies are charged at 18%, so the seller collects VAT and may claim eligible input VAT.
  • Zero-rated supplies are charged at 0%, which can still preserve input tax recovery where the law allows.
  • Exempt supplies do not attract VAT, but input tax recovery is usually restricted.

For a trading business, a service firm, or an online seller, the first question is rarely "what is the rate?" The real first question is usually "do I already need to register?" That is where Uganda's threshold tests matter more than almost anything else.

The UGX 150 million and UGX 37.5 million registration tests

URA guidance and the VAT law use two main tests that businesses should monitor continuously. If either one is crossed, the business should stop treating VAT as optional.

Registration test Current level What it means in practice
Annual turnover test UGX 150,000,000 If taxable supplies exceed UGX 150 million in twelve months, VAT registration is required.
Short-period test UGX 37,500,000 If taxable supplies exceed UGX 37.5 million in any three consecutive calendar months, VAT registration is required.
Voluntary registration Case by case URA can accept voluntary registration, but businesses should be ready with proper records, premises details, and a real taxable activity.

The annual test is straightforward, but the three-month test is where businesses often get caught late. A company can stay below UGX 150 million on a trailing annual basis and still trigger VAT registration because it has a fast quarter. Seasonal businesses, import-heavy traders, agencies with one large contract, and event businesses are the usual candidates.

  • Do not track only annual turnover. Uganda's three-month test can trigger registration much earlier.
  • Use taxable supplies, not profit. VAT registration looks at turnover from taxable activity, not how much money you kept after costs.
  • Review every month. By the time you prepare year-end accounts, you may already be late on VAT compliance.

How VAT registration works with URA

Once the business is within the VAT net, the cleanest approach is to think of registration as a workflow rather than a single form. VAT registration depends on your broader tax and business records being coherent first.

Step What to prepare Why it matters
1 TIN and entity details Your URA tax account has to exist before VAT can sit on top of it. If you still need a tax identity, start with the Uganda TIN guide.
2 URSB and business records Companies and formal businesses should have consistent registration records, addresses, and activity descriptions.
3 Turnover evidence Keep invoices, contracts, sales ledgers, and bank support showing why the business falls into compulsory or voluntary registration.
4 Tax portal setup VAT is not just about registration. You will need a return filing rhythm immediately afterwards.
5 EFRIS preparation URA says VAT-registered taxpayers are obliged to register for EFRIS, so invoicing should be planned early.

Businesses sometimes treat VAT registration as something to fix after growth has already happened. That is backwards. Once the threshold is crossed, invoice design, pricing, customer contracts, and input VAT support all need to line up. A business that starts charging VAT late often ends up bearing part of the tax from its own margin because customer prices were quoted without VAT.

If your business is still being formalised, it is worth checking the surrounding compliance pieces as well, especially your Uganda business licence requirements and your URA account setup. VAT works best when those records agree with each other.

EFRIS, invoicing, and record keeping

URA's published guidance for 2024/25 states that all VAT-registered taxpayers are obliged to register for EFRIS. In practical terms, that means VAT compliance is now tightly connected to electronic fiscal invoicing. If your business is registered for VAT but your invoicing process is still informal, you are already creating avoidable risk.

EFRIS matters for more than optics. It helps determine whether your invoices and transaction records support the VAT position you declare. A business claiming input VAT without clean purchase support, or issuing inconsistent sales documentation, creates exactly the kind of mismatch URA systems are designed to surface.

At minimum, a VAT-ready process should include:

  • VAT invoices that clearly separate the taxable value and the VAT amount.
  • Purchase records that support any input VAT claim.
  • Sales summaries that reconcile to returns and banking records.
  • Product or service mapping so exempt, zero-rated, and standard-rated transactions are not mixed carelessly.
  • Monthly reviews before the filing deadline instead of after it.

This is also the point where many businesses discover they need a clearer internal rule about what they sell. If you make both taxable and exempt supplies, the classification issue becomes operational, not academic. The wrong supply coding can distort the VAT you charge and the input tax you think you can recover.

Monthly returns and payment deadlines

Uganda VAT returns are filed monthly. URA's filing guidance states that the return is due by the 15th day of the month following the tax period, and the payment due date is the same. That means a March VAT period is due on April 15, an April VAT period is due on May 15, and so on.

This point is important enough to say plainly because it is easy to get wrong when businesses copy old notes or generic East African VAT summaries. The filing date to work with is the 15th, not the 20th or the 21st.

Practical filing rule: close your sales and purchase records a few days before the 15th. Businesses that wait until the deadline day usually end up filing with incomplete invoices or unresolved customer-credit issues.

Monthly VAT discipline usually comes down to four questions:

  1. What output VAT did we charge this month?
  2. What input VAT is supportable this month?
  3. Do our invoices and records agree with the return?
  4. Is the payment ready by the 15th?

Even when a month is quiet, the business should still decide explicitly whether it has a nil return or a normal return. Silence is not a filing strategy.

Zero-rated vs exempt supplies

Many VAT errors in Uganda happen because businesses understand the 18% rate but treat zero-rated and exempt supplies as if they were the same thing. They are not.

Supply type Typical treatment Examples often cited in guidance
Standard-rated VAT charged at 18% Most ordinary taxable domestic goods and services
Zero-rated VAT charged at 0% Exports and some water supplies referenced in the VAT schedules
Exempt No VAT charged Common exempt categories include some education, health, financial services, and unprocessed food items

Why does the distinction matter? Because a 0% supply can still sit inside the VAT system, while an exempt supply usually sits outside normal input tax recovery. That difference affects margin, refunds, and whether the business is keeping the right support.

If you sell a mixed basket of goods or services, do not rely on a rough mental list. Go back to the statutory schedules and the URA guidance for your exact supply. A business can be fully compliant on one product line and fully wrong on the next.

Threshold and filing walkthroughs

The examples below are not fictional business stories. They are simple formula walkthroughs based on the current published thresholds and filing rules.

Walkthrough 1: The annual turnover test

A trader records taxable sales of UGX 12 million per month from May 2025 to April 2026. Twelve months of sales at UGX 12 million equals UGX 144 million. On that fact pattern alone, the annual registration test has not yet been crossed.

If the same trader's sales rise to UGX 13 million in May 2026, the rolling twelve-month total becomes UGX 145 million if one UGX 12 million month drops out, or more depending on the month sequence. Once the rolling twelve-month total goes beyond UGX 150 million, the VAT registration question stops being theoretical.

Walkthrough 2: The three-month test catches growth earlier

Now take a service business that bills:

  • January: UGX 14 million
  • February: UGX 12 million
  • March: UGX 13 million

The three consecutive months total UGX 39 million. That is above the UGX 37.5 million short-period test, even though the business may still be below UGX 150 million on a full-year basis. This is exactly why fast-growing businesses should review turnover every month, not just at year end.

Walkthrough 3: Monthly filing date

If your April 2026 VAT period includes standard-rated sales of UGX 20 million, the output VAT at 18% is UGX 3.6 million. If eligible input VAT for the same month is UGX 1.1 million, the net VAT position is UGX 2.5 million. The return and payment for that April period are due by May 15, 2026.

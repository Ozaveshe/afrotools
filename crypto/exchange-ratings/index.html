Trust Score Methodology

Our trust score is a weighted composite of five categories, each independently verified. The maximum score is 100.

⚖

Regulatory Compliance

Up to 30 points

Does the exchange hold licenses in recognized jurisdictions? Is it registered with financial regulators in the African countries it operates in? Higher scores for multiple licenses and transparent compliance reporting.

🕑

Operating History

Up to 20 points

How long has the exchange been operating? Has it survived market cycles? Were there any security breaches, and if so, were affected users compensated? Longer track records with clean histories score highest.

👥

Community Trust

Up to 20 points

Aggregated user reviews, social media sentiment, and app store ratings from African users. We weight reviews from verified users in African countries more heavily than global scores.

🔒

Proof of Reserves

Up to 15 points

Does the exchange publish proof-of-reserves audits? Are reserves verified by independent third parties? Exchanges with on-chain proof-of-reserves and regular attestation reports score highest.

🛡

Insurance & Protection

Up to 15 points

Does the exchange maintain an insurance fund to cover losses from hacks or operational failures? SAFU funds, cold storage policies, and user asset protection mechanisms are evaluated.

How to Choose a Safe Crypto Exchange in Africa

Choosing a cryptocurrency exchange is one of the most consequential decisions any African crypto user will make. Unlike traditional banking, where deposits are typically insured by government agencies, crypto exchanges operate in a regulatory grey area across much of the continent. If an exchange gets hacked, goes bankrupt, or simply decides to freeze your account, your funds may be gone with no legal recourse. This is why trust should be your primary consideration, not just fees or coin selection.

The African crypto market has exploded in recent years. Nigeria consistently ranks among the top five countries globally for crypto adoption, while Kenya, South Africa, Ghana, and Tanzania have all seen massive growth in peer-to-peer trading volumes. But this growth has also attracted bad actors. Ponzi schemes disguised as crypto platforms have cost African users billions of dollars. Fly-by-night exchanges have appeared, collected deposits, and vanished. Even legitimate international platforms have sometimes failed African users by restricting access to local banking rails or imposing sudden KYC requirements that lock users out of their own funds.

Regulatory compliance is arguably the most important factor when selecting an exchange. In South Africa, the Financial Sector Conduct Authority (FSCA) now requires crypto asset service providers to be licensed. Exchanges like Luno have proactively obtained these licenses, demonstrating a commitment to operating within the law. In Nigeria, the Securities and Exchange Commission is developing a regulatory framework, and exchanges that engage constructively with regulators tend to be more trustworthy than those that operate entirely offshore with no local presence.

Proof of reserves has become a critical trust indicator since the collapse of FTX in November 2022. Before FTX imploded, it was considered one of the most reputable exchanges in the world. The lesson for African users is clear: reputation alone is not enough. You should prioritize exchanges that publish cryptographic proof-of-reserves, showing that they hold enough assets to cover all customer deposits. Binance and Bybit both provide on-chain proof-of-reserves that anyone can verify independently.

Security history matters, but context is important. KuCoin suffered a hack in 2020 but recovered all stolen funds and compensated every affected user. That response actually demonstrated strong security practices and financial reserves. Compare this to exchanges that have been hacked and never recovered user funds. A clean security record is ideal, but how an exchange responds to incidents reveals more about its trustworthiness than whether incidents have occurred at all.

For African users specifically, deposit and withdrawal options are a practical safety concern. An exchange might have excellent security and low fees, but if the only way to withdraw funds is through an international wire transfer that costs fifty dollars and takes five business days, it effectively traps your money. The best exchanges for African users support local bank transfers, mobile money where applicable, and maintain reliable NGN, KES, ZAR, and GHS on-ramps. P2P platforms add flexibility but introduce counterparty risk, so look for exchanges with robust escrow systems and dispute resolution processes.

Finally, diversification is a fundamental risk management principle that applies to exchange selection as well. Do not keep all your crypto on a single exchange, no matter how trustworthy it appears. Split your holdings across two or three reputable platforms, and consider moving long-term holdings to a personal hardware wallet where you control the private keys. Our trust scores above are designed to help you identify the most reliable options, but no exchange is entirely risk-free. Stay informed, verify claims independently, and never invest more than you can afford to lose.
